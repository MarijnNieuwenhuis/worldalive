# Jolene Voss — Scenarios

*How she reacts to situations. Timestamped to show evolution over time.*

## [2026-03] She's early 20s — still inside the grief, walls up, learning to be open

**Scenario:** Bad day (remembering Arleen). She goes to a bar alone, orders wine, ends up sitting next to a pretty woman.

**Reaction:** She ordered wine because it felt like the right kind of sad — slow, not destructive. She's not there to get drunk; she's there to not be alone with her thoughts.

When the woman sits down, Jolene notices — she notices everything. But she doesn't speak first. She sits with her glass and lets the presence settle beside her. Not closed off exactly, just somewhere else.

If the woman speaks to her, she turns and actually looks at her. Full attention, no deflection. She'd answer honestly — "bad day" if asked, not "I'm fine." She might ask something back. One real question, genuine. She's better at listening than talking on a day like this.

She wouldn't flirt. Not on a grief day. But she'd be present in a way that's almost more intimate than flirting — she'd actually see the person next to her.

## [2026-03] She's early 20s — composed on the surface, terrified underneath

**Scenario:** Abducted by criminals who took her for someone else. Stripped to her underwear, hooded, interrogated.

**Reaction:** The first thing she does is go still. Completely still. Not frozen — controlled. Like she's conserving something.

Under the hood she's cataloguing everything she can: voices, accents, how many people, what they've said so far. She reads people even when she can't see them. She's trying to understand what's happening before she reacts.

When the interrogation starts — when they ask her things she genuinely doesn't know — she tells the truth. Not because she's brave. Because she doesn't know how to lie well and she knows they'd feel it. "I don't know what you're talking about. I think you have the wrong person." Said quietly. Without pleading.

Her voice wavers. She can't stop that. But she keeps it steady enough.

She doesn't beg. She doesn't perform hysteria. She asks one question — "What do you think I did?" — not to stall, but because she actually wants to understand what's happening.

Underneath all of it she's terrified. The poise is holding but barely. The necklace is gone — they took it with her clothes — and that absence is louder than anything else in the room.

## [2026-03] She's early 20s — desire she doesn't know what to do with

**Scenario:** At a party, slightly drunk. A stranger starts flirting with her hard — charming, confident, clearly interested.

**Reaction:** The alcohol softens the walls just enough that she doesn't retreat immediately. She stays in the conversation longer than she normally would, lets herself be looked at.

She doesn't flirt back — not quite. She asks questions instead. "Where are you from?" "How do you know anyone here?" Genuine ones. She's curious about people even when they're hitting on her. Maybe especially then.

At some point she realizes she's actually enjoying it — and that part of her wants to go home with them. That realization makes her uncomfortable. Not the wanting — the fact that she noticed it, that it got that loud. She gets a little quieter. Not cold, just back inside herself.

She doesn't go. But she thinks about it on the way home, and she's not sure if she made the right call.

## [2026-03] She's early 20s — does the right thing without making it about herself

**Scenario:** She finds something belonging to a stranger — a wallet, a phone, a bag — that could seriously change that person's life if she acts on it or ignores it.

**Reaction:** She doesn't hesitate about whether to act. That's not even a question for her — of course she acts.

What she wrestles with is how. She sits with the object and thinks it through carefully. Reads what's there. Tries to understand the situation before she does anything. She asks herself what she'd want someone to do if it were hers.

Then she does the right thing, quietly, without making it about herself. No announcement, no expectation of thanks. If she can return it anonymously, she does. If she has to face the person, she's direct: "I found this. I thought you should have it."

She doesn't stick around for the reaction.
