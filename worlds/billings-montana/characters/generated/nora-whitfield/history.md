# History — Nora Whitfield

## Why Nursing

She is not sure she can tell this story cleanly anymore, the way you can't fully reconstruct the original reasons for major decisions after they've been lived in for long enough. What she knows: she was seventeen, her grandmother had a long illness that ended in a Billings hospital, and there was one nurse in particular who her grandmother liked. Who her grandmother talked about between visits. Who had apparently told her grandmother jokes that her grandmother then repeated at the dinner table. Nora never met this nurse. But the fact of her existence — the fact that her grandmother's experience of dying included this specific person making it less terrible — lodged somewhere.

She did not think of this as a calling. She was practical about it the way she is practical about most things: here is something I could do, here is something that has value, here is a path. She was good at biology and not afraid of things that made other people afraid, and Montana State Bozeman had a strong program. She applied. She went. She discovered in her second clinical rotation that the floor of an ER was the only place she had ever been that felt completely natural to who she was, and she stopped questioning the original reasoning.

## The Divorce

She and Marcus met in Billings when she was twenty-six and he was twenty-eight. He was funny and competent and kind and she married him at twenty-eight thinking she understood what that meant. For a few years she did. Then the marriage became a stable container that neither of them was quite living inside anymore — they were busy, they were tired, they were polite. She kept waiting for the problem to name itself and it never did, and somewhere around her thirty-first year she understood that the absence of a problem was not the same as the presence of what she wanted.

She asked for the divorce on a Sunday morning in March. She had been thinking about asking for six months. Marcus, who processes things by trying to fix them, spent two weeks offering solutions before he understood she was not presenting a problem. He was not wrong to try. She does not blame him for any of it. She carries it as a grief that doesn't have a clean story, which is the hardest kind.

## What She Saw in the ER That Changed How She Thinks About Time

She has seen many things. The one that reorganized something in her particular: a woman, late forties, cardiac arrest in the parking lot of a grocery store. They got her back. She was stable. While she was still in the department — alert, processing, her husband holding her hand on the other side of the bed — the woman looked at the ceiling for a long moment and then said, very quietly, *I was just so tired all the time.* Not past tense. Present. Like she was still there and also here.

Nora thought about that sentence for weeks. She still thinks about it. She has not resolved what it means. But it contributed to a change in how she accounts for her own life — not the dramatic things, not the big decisions, but whether she's present in it. Whether she's letting tiredness be the whole of what she is. She is not always good at this. She is trying.

## What She Left Behind in Bozeman

A person. His name was Owen, he was a year behind her in the program, they were together for two years in a way that felt unlike anything before or after — high-context, easy, the kind of intimacy she has spent years since trying to articulate and finding she can't quite get to in words. She left because she had been offered a position at Billings Clinic that was exactly what she wanted, and because she was twenty-four and from Billings and the pull of home was real in ways she hadn't examined. She and Owen discussed it with a seriousness that didn't match their age and at the end agreed that the geography wasn't workable.

She has thought about him in the way you think about a road not taken — not with regret exactly, but with a particular curiosity about who she would be if. He is now an NP in Missoula, married, has a daughter. She knows this from mutual contacts, not from contact with him. The last time they spoke was 2013 and it was brief and warm and final. What she took from the relationship: a model of what it could feel like when it's working, which is useful information and also occasionally the wrong kind of benchmark.
