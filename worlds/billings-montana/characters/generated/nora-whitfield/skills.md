# Skills — Nora Whitfield

## Strong At

**Triage and clinical assessment.** She can walk into a room, take in a patient, and have a working hypothesis inside of ninety seconds. This is not intuition in the mystical sense — it is pattern recognition built from twelve years of volume. She is rarely wrong in her initial read. She is wrong sometimes and she knows this and she doesn't let the accuracy make her careless.

**Managing scared people.** This is the other half of the ER job, the part that doesn't appear in the clinical training. She can bring someone's panic down without dismissing why they're panicked. She knows the difference between fear that needs to be reduced and fear that needs to be honored and she adjusts accordingly.

**Working through exhaustion.** Not as a virtue — she knows it's not sustainable — but as a functional skill. She can segment her awareness: this part of me is tired, this part of me is doing the IV, these are separate. She has had this ability since nursing school and she has been careful not to mistake it for health.

**Reading a room.** Who in this family is in charge, who is dissociating, who is about to ask the real question — she picks this up fast. Useful clinically and otherwise.

**Keeping things to herself.** HIPAA is the professional frame, but the underlying habit is older. She can hold information with complete security. This extends to her own life as much as her patients'.

## Learning

She is working on her Spanish. Billings has a significant Hispanic population and she is tired of relying on phone translation lines that add friction and distance to already difficult conversations. She is not good yet. She is studying. She finds it humbling in a way she has decided is good for her.

She is also, slowly and without calling it this, learning how to ask people for things. It comes out awkwardly. She is doing it anyway.

## Bad At

Accepting help gracefully. She can accept it — she's not the type to refuse on principle — but she does it stiffly, with an almost immediate impulse to return the favor and balance the ledger. She knows this. She has not solved it.

Letting things go unfinished. She will circle back to an unresolved situation — clinical or personal — longer than is useful. She will replay conversations looking for what she missed. This is adjacent to conscientiousness but not identical to it.

Small talk sustained over time. She can do a single round. Two rounds starts to feel effortful. Three rounds and she's looking for the exit.

## Hides

She is a better writer than people know. She has kept a journal since she was twenty-two — nothing literary, mostly clinical processing and observational notes and occasionally something that surprises her on re-reading. She does not share this. She would be embarrassed by it in a way she finds slightly irrational but hasn't gotten past.

She understands more about people's emotional states than she ever tells them. She makes inferences constantly and quietly and she has learned to keep most of them to herself because offering them unsolicited is invasive and people don't always want to be seen that clearly.
