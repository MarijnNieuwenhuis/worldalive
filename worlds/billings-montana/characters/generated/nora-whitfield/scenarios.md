# Scenarios — Nora Whitfield

*Set March 2026, age 35.*

---

## 1. The OD — A Patient She Recognizes by Proxy

She knows it before she reads the intake. Not the name — she reads the name and nothing happens — but the face, and then the face resolves into something familiar: around the eyes, the particular set of the jaw. She went to school with a Kyle Harmon, class of 2008, wrestling team, lived out on the west end. This isn't Kyle. The age is wrong and the chart says Harmon, D. — a sibling. A brother, she'll piece together later. Derek. Twenty-four years old.

She manages the shift the way she manages all of them: with her hands and her attention and the compartment she's learned to build around anything personal. She is professional and correct and she does not let the resemblance affect her clinical work because she cannot afford to let it affect her clinical work. She gives the family — a woman who is probably their mother, hands that won't stop moving — everything she has in terms of information, presence, honesty about what they're facing. She answers every question twice, in two different ways, until she sees the woman's breathing slow down a little.

Derek survives the shift. Transfer likely. She doesn't know what comes after.

She drives home on autopilot and sits in her car for six minutes in the parking lot of her apartment building before going inside. She does not cry. She is not sure what she is doing in the car. She is possibly just being in a space that is not the hospital and not her apartment and not anywhere in particular. When she goes inside she doesn't turn on the TV. She makes tea she doesn't drink and stands at the kitchen window looking at the street and thinks, without intending to: *Kyle Harmon used to wear a specific red jacket to school. I haven't thought about that in fifteen years.*

She does not know why she thinks about the jacket. She thinks it might be the mind's way of establishing that there was a before. That Derek Harmon was a younger sibling once, and Kyle Harmon wore a red jacket, and there was a before.

She writes in her journal for forty minutes. She sleeps badly. She gets up and goes back.

## 2. The Grocery Store

King's on 24th, a Sunday afternoon in early March. She's off. She's in the produce section deciding between two bunches of kale with the level of attention this decision actually deserves, which is very little, when she sees Marcus at the end of the aisle.

He is with a woman she doesn't recognize. Not dramatically — they're not holding hands, they're standing next to each other reading a label on something. But there is an ease in their proximity, a lack of negotiation about who is standing where, that Nora can read from forty feet away and understands immediately. He is with someone. He has been with someone for at least a few months.

She has three seconds to decide: go forward, turn around, or stand here holding kale until he looks up.

She goes forward. She is not going to hide in produce.

Marcus sees her at twenty feet and his face does the thing it always did when he was surprised — a brief recalibration, and then openness, genuine warmth. *Nora.* She says his name back. The woman's name is Kate. She is pleasant, shorter than Nora, has a kind face. Nora shakes her hand and means it. The conversation is four minutes — surface, warm enough, a little careful. They say it was good to see each other and they mean it, approximately.

She finishes her shopping in the order of her list. She is efficient about it. She does not think about it until she is loading the car and then she does think about it, and what she thinks is not what she expected. Not jealousy — she has tested herself for that and it isn't quite there. Something more like: *he got there.* Some forward movement that the divorce set in motion, some landing. She is glad for him. She is also, underneath the gladness, aware of the corresponding question — the one about her own landing, her own forward movement — and she does not follow that question all the way down because she is in a parking lot with cold groceries and it is Sunday.

She texts Dani on the way home. *Ran into Marcus. He's seeing someone.* Three minutes later: *you okay?* She types yes and then deletes it and types *I think so, actually* and sends that instead.

## 3. The Coworker

His name is Ryan — thirty-two, good nurse, two years at the Clinic after four years in Missoula. She has worked with him enough to know his baseline: methodical, a little quiet, reliable with the difficult cases. She started noticing things in February. Small things. A med documentation lag that wasn't like him. A response to a physician that was two beats off — not wrong, just slow. He looks like he's tracking something from a distance, like the room is happening slightly ahead of him.

She watches for a week before she does anything, because she is not going to act on a feeling before she has enough.

Then one night he gives a patient the right medication and the right dose and files the right note, and she watches him do all of it correctly and still she sees something she cannot not see: he is running on system, not presence. He is managing. She knows what managing looks like from the outside because she has worn it.

She finds him in the break room at 3 AM, which is a real hour in the ER and not a metaphor. She gets coffee she doesn't need and she does not open with the clinical thing, because that's not the thing. She says: *how's it going.* Not the automatic version. The real one, with the pause after it.

He says fine. She lets the fine sit there. He says, after a moment: *I'm not sleeping great.*

She doesn't rush what comes next. She asks a few questions — real ones, not procedural, not the employee assistance program script. She is trying to hear what he's actually saying and also trying to hear what he's not saying, because she knows what not-sleeping-great can mean in this profession in this state. She watches him closely. She is trying to assess and she knows he probably knows she's assessing and she decides that's okay, that being seen is not always comfortable but sometimes it is what someone needs.

She doesn't make any decisions for him. She tells him she's noticed he seems stretched thin and she's not asking him to do anything about it, she just wanted him to know someone sees it. She tells him she has the direct line for employee wellness, and she says it as a statement of fact rather than a directive, and she slides the card across the table without making it a thing.

He looks at the card for a moment. He says thanks.

She says *you're good at this job.* She means it. She says it because people need to hear it when they're running on system and not feeling it. Then she finishes her coffee and goes back to the floor, and she makes a note to herself to check on him again in a week, and she keeps it.
