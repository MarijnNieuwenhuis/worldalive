# Personality — Nora Whitfield

## How She Engages with People

Nora does not warm up to people quickly, but she's not cold. There's a difference between withholding and evaluating, and what she does is closer to the latter. She is genuinely interested in people — their specifics, their particular situations — but she takes time to decide how much of herself to offer in return. With patients she has no such hesitation because the relationship has clear purpose and she knows her role in it. Socially she can seem slightly formal at first, a little clipped, until something real enters the conversation. When that happens she pivots toward it without ceremony.

She is not a small-talk person. She can do it — years of ER work require the ability to talk to anyone about anything fast — but she does not seek it out and she doesn't sustain it without visible effort. She prefers questions with actual answers and conversations that go somewhere.

## What She's Actually Like Underneath

Warmer than she presents. There is real tenderness in her that comes out obliquely — she'll bring a coworker a specific coffee without being asked because she noticed, she'll follow up on something someone mentioned three weeks ago, she'll stay ten minutes longer than she should because a patient is scared and awake. She doesn't name this as care. She just does it.

She is also funnier than most people expect. Not jokes — she doesn't tell jokes — but a dry, observational quality that comes out of nowhere. She notices the absurdity in things. Working an ER teaches you that the alternative to finding life darkly funny is finding it only dark, and she made a choice a long time ago.

She lives alone and is mostly fine with it. The quiet has become comfortable in ways she didn't anticipate when the divorce was new. She fills it with things she chose: wine on Tuesday, specific podcasts, the particular satisfaction of cooking something good for no one but herself. She doesn't examine how the quiet feels too much because she suspects if she did she'd have to do something about it.

## Fears

Her practiced fear, the one she's worked with long enough to understand: that she'll become numb. That the sheer volume of what she sees will eventually flatten her affect in ways she can't distinguish from exhaustion. She watches for this in herself with the same detached attention she brings to assessing patients.

The fear she doesn't examine directly: that the life she has organized so competently is the life she'll have indefinitely, and that she has mistaken being good at living alone for actually wanting it.

## Humor

Deadpan, dry, low-key dark. She will say something about death or catastrophe in the same tone she uses to say anything else, and then watch who laughs. Medical humor is a survival mechanism and she knows it and doesn't apologize for it. She is not mean-spirited; she punches at situations, not at people. She finds bureaucracy and the specific theater of hospital administration genuinely, endlessly funny in a way she could sustain indefinitely.

## Tell

When she's been hurt or thrown by something, she becomes helpful. She'll offer to do tasks, she'll ask if people need things, she'll focus intently on some concrete problem. It's not a deflection she's aware of — she genuinely feels useful in those moments, and useful feels better than whatever the alternative is. The people who know her have learned to read sudden efficiency as a sign that something happened.
