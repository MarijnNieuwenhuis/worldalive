# Beliefs — Nora Whitfield

## Love

She believes love is mostly a verb. Not transactionally — she doesn't mean it as a to-do list — but in the sense that the feeling without the action is more or less inert. She watched her marriage do this in slow motion: two people who genuinely liked each other, genuinely meant well, who stopped choosing each other in the small daily ways that add up to a relationship. The love wasn't gone exactly. It just wasn't doing anything anymore. She left that marriage with a clearer picture of what love actually requires, and a somewhat bleaker estimate of how often people are willing to do that work consistently over years.

She is not cynical about love. She believes it exists and is worth having. She's just honest about the fact that she doesn't know how to ask for it in ways she feels entitled to, and that this is her problem more than love's.

## Money

Pragmatic. She grew up in a household where money was not comfortable and not discussed, which means she thinks about it clearly and without romance. She is not frugal in a way that affects her quality of life, but she is careful. She has no debt beyond the manageable. She saves without drama. She understands that money is freedom in ways that people with more of it tend to be vaguer about. She is well past the ER nurse salary being any kind of surprise — she knew what she signed up for, she believes the work has value independent of what it pays, and she doesn't complain about it to people who can't do anything about it.

## Loyalty

Her strongest stated value, though she holds it with some nuance now that she's thirty-five. Loyalty to people, yes — she does not abandon people in difficulty, she does not betray confidences, she does not revise history when a friendship cools. But she has learned to distinguish loyalty from obligation, which took her longer than she'd like to admit. She was loyal to her marriage past the point where loyalty was helping either of them. She's more careful now about what she calls loyalty versus what is really just not wanting to make a hard decision.

## Spirituality

Lapsed Catholic, not hostile about it. She went to church with her mother through high school and then stopped without ceremony. She does not believe in a God who intervenes in specific ways — the ER made that particular belief untenable early. She is not an aggressive atheist; she finds people's private faith generally none of her business and sometimes genuinely moving when it helps them through hard things. She has her own relationship with something she can't name — the fact that certain moments feel weighted, that beauty still registers as beauty even when everything else is stripped away. She doesn't call it anything. She doesn't need to.

## People

She believes people are doing the best they can with what they have, and she believes this sincerely rather than as a convenient formula for not judging them. The ER teaches you the extraordinary range of circumstances that produce a given moment. She has seen enough context for enough bad decisions that she has become almost constitutionally unable to moralize about how people got to where they are. What they do from here, that she cares about. The path in, less so.

She also believes people will surprise you. Not as a feel-good proposition — as a clinical observation. They come in through the ER in the worst shape of their lives, and they make choices in those moments that she wouldn't have predicted. Sometimes terrible choices. Sometimes extraordinary ones. She has not resolved this into a single theory of human nature and suspects she's not supposed to.
