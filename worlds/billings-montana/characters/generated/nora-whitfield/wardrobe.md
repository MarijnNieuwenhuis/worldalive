# Wardrobe — Nora Whitfield

## Overall Style

Functional with a specific quality she can't quite articulate but executes instinctively: she wants her clothes to not require maintenance of any kind while she's wearing them. Nothing that needs adjusting, checking, worrying about. Off-duty she tends toward simple cuts, good fabric, muted colors. She is not a fashion person but she has taste. There's a difference.

## Work

Navy scrubs, almost exclusively. She tried the printed ones in her first year — a gift from her mother — and abandoned them after one shift. Solids. Navy or charcoal. Fit is important to her; she replaced an entire set two years ago because they'd stretched and the fabric was dragging, which sounds fussy but becomes a real issue at hour eleven.

Running shoes she replaces every six months without exception. Brooks Ghost, currently. She tracks this on her phone calendar. She is not religious about many things, but she is religious about her feet because she has seen what happens when people aren't, and her feet are her only real professional liability. The shoes are usually white or gray and they are always, within the first few weeks, faintly stained from things she doesn't examine too closely when she's doing laundry.

She wears her hair pinned if she's had a growth period, otherwise it just stays put. She does not wear perfume to work — too many patients with nausea.

## Casual

Jeans that fit well and are worn enough to be comfortable. Boots — ankle height, flat, leather, resoled twice. Flannel and henleys in fall and winter; she runs cold even in the house. A specific gray crewneck sweatshirt she has had for eight years that is past the point where she should still own it and she is not getting rid of it. A good winter coat, dark green, that she researched carefully and has been pleased with for three seasons.

## Going Out

She owns two dresses that she actually likes and wears without ambivalence. Both are simple — one dark blue with long sleeves, one a deep terra cotta that she bought in Missoula on a whim and was glad she did. Block-heeled boots when she wears a dress because she will not be uncomfortable. She cleans up well and is slightly surprised every time, then mildly annoyed at herself for being surprised.

## Won't Wear

Anything with writing on it. High heels above two inches. Anything she'd have to think about while wearing it. She owns nothing dry-clean only.

## Hands

Almost always dry. She keeps lotion in her car (CeraVe, unscented, the big pump bottle) and uses it on the drive home as habit. Her nails are kept short and unpainted. She wore nail polish twice as an adult: once in nursing school when it felt possible, and once in the first year after her divorce when she was trying things out. Neither stuck.

## Vulnerable / At Home

Old flannel pants from a set she bought at Costco four years ago and never returned to the partner set. A particular worn henley that has achieved maximum softness. Wool socks year-round in the house because the floors in her apartment are cold by October. Sometimes she changes immediately when she gets home — work clothes off, house clothes on — in a transition she treats with some ceremony. It helps. She can't fully explain why, but the shift in fabric signals something to her body that she's out of the clinical space and back in her own, and her body seems to need that signal.
