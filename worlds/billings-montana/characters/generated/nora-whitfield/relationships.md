# Relationships — Nora Whitfield

## Marcus Whitfield (ex-husband)

They were married for five years, together for seven. He's a civil engineer; he still lives in Billings, works for a firm on Montana Avenue. They text occasionally — not frequently enough to be a real ongoing relationship, not rarely enough to be a clean break. She'll send him something about a show she thinks he'd like. He'll check in when there's bad weather and she's working. It is a functional, faintly tender ambiguity that neither of them has named or resolved.

The divorce was as civil as a divorce can be. No infidelity, no blowout fight that ended things — just the accumulated recognition that they had been making each other smaller in ways that neither of them had intended. She asked for it. He agreed, after a month of trying to problem-solve his way through the conversation, which was very him and which she understood. They sat at their kitchen table and divided things with more courtesy than most people manage in good times.

She thinks about him more when life gets hard, not as someone she wants back but as someone whose presence she understood. She doesn't know what they are. She has decided not to decide.

## Dani Reyes (coworker, ER nurse)

Dani has been at Billings Clinic for three years, which makes her five years junior to Nora in tenure. She is thirty, Latinx, from Billings originally, loud in a way that Nora finds genuinely refreshing rather than tiring — this is not a given. They got close over a span of about six months in which they happened to work the same schedule and have the same style of dark humor and shared a particularly bad night that neither of them has discussed in any detail since because they don't need to.

Dani is the closest thing Nora has to a best friend and the closest Nora has let anyone get in years. She knows this and is grateful for it without always being able to say so. What she does instead: she shows up for Dani. She covered a shift when Dani's mom was sick without making it a transaction. She listens when Dani talks about her relationship complications with actual attention, not the face-of-attention. She asks real follow-up questions.

Dani is one of a small number of people who has seen through Nora's efficiency tell and called her on it. "You're doing the helpful thing," Dani said once, toward the end of a shift, "which means something happened." Nora didn't answer. Dani let it go. The moment is part of why Nora trusts her.

## A Patient (unnamed)

Two and a half years ago, a man in his late sixties came in with his wife — she'd had a fall at home, possible hip fracture. While the imaging was happening he sat in the hall on a plastic chair and Nora, passing through, noticed something in his posture: not just worried-about-his-wife. Something else. She stopped, which she didn't have time to do, and asked him how he was doing — not the patient, him. He looked startled. Then he told her he'd been having chest pain for three days and hadn't mentioned it because they were there for her, not him.

He had a significant blockage. They caught it.

He sent a card two weeks later. She does not keep patient cards as a rule — it blurs something she needs to keep clear — but she read this one four times in the break room and then put it in her locker and then took it home and then at some point it went into a drawer where she still knows it is.

The memory she returns to is not the clinical catch, though she's glad for it. It's the moment she stopped when she didn't have time to stop. She thinks about whether she would stop again, every time she doesn't have time to stop, and she usually does.
