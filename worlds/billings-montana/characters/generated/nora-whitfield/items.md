# Items — Nora Whitfield

## What She Carries

**Her work badge.** Billings Clinic ID, laminated, worn on a retractable clip that she keeps on the left hip of her scrubs. Her photo is from four years ago — she looks slightly younger, her hair is a little longer. She has been meaning to get it updated. She hasn't. The photo is a version of her she still recognizes, which might be part of it.

**Her phone.** A three-year-old iPhone in a plain black case. She does not have many apps. She has her Spanish learning app, which she opens more often than she completes a lesson. She has a notes app where she keeps track of things she wants to look up later — clinical and otherwise — and this list is long and not well-organized and she finds it oddly comforting to have. Her home screen is a photo of the Rimrocks at dusk, taken from the parking lot of a gas station on 6th Street the fall she came back to Billings. She took it without planning to and it turned out well.

**A small tube of hand lotion in her jacket pocket.** Not her car tube — a backup. Unscented. She has learned to keep this on her person in dry weather months, which in Montana is most months.

**Her keys, which include** a key to her apartment, a key to her car, and a flat metal keychain that says YELLOWSTONE in block letters from a trip she took alone in 2023. She went by herself and did not tell many people she was going alone because she didn't want the conversation about it. It was one of the better three days she's had in recent memory.

## Something from the Divorce or Before It

In her bedside drawer: a postcard. Her ex-husband, Marcus, sent it from a work conference in Seattle two months before she asked for the divorce — a shot of Pike Place Market, handwritten on the back in his cramped scrawl: *thought you'd like this. hope your week is okay.* She kept it. She is not sure what it means that she kept it. She doesn't look at it often, but she knows it's there, and knowing it's there is the point. It is evidence of something — not that the marriage was good enough to save, but that it was real, that there was tenderness in it, that she didn't imagine the whole thing.

## Something That Contradicts Her Controlled Exterior

On her kitchen counter: a small ceramic planter shaped like a bear, chipped at the ear, painted in shades of blue and rust. She bought it at a craft fair on 4th Street three years ago on an impulsive Saturday morning when she had nowhere to be. She was slightly embarrassed at the register. She keeps a succulent in it that she has managed not to kill. The bear sits next to her coffee maker where she sees it every morning, and she has never once considered removing it. She does not know why she bought it. She doesn't need to.
