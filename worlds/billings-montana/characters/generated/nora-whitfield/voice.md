# Voice — Nora Whitfield

## Register and Pace

Lower end of the female register, not deep but grounded. She doesn't rush. Her pace is measured in a way that reads as confidence to most people — she's not performing certainty, she's simply not filling space out of anxiety. Standard Western American vowels, the flat prairie version: "bag" doesn't become "beg," "caught" and "cot" are the same sound. No particular Montana twang beyond that baseline. The accent is mostly eroded from nursing school years in Bozeman and from spending twelve-hour shifts explaining things carefully to people from everywhere.

## Delivery

Dry. She will say something genuinely funny with complete seriousness and wait. Not performing deadpan — she just doesn't feel the need to signal that something is a joke. If you catch it, good. If you don't, that's interesting information too. She's not cruel about this; she reads the room for whether someone needs the signal and will provide it then. With people she trusts she'll drop even that.

She uses pauses deliberately. Not nervous pauses or space-filling "um" — actual silence where she's finishing a thought or deciding something. The pause is part of the communication. New people sometimes try to fill it. She lets them.

## Under Stress

Her voice flattens rather than rises. Volume stays almost exactly the same. If anything, she gets quieter and more precise in high-stakes situations — this is the ER training, the way specificity becomes survival. People who expect distress to read as distress sometimes miss when she's in it. Her flatness under pressure is not an absence of feeling; it's a professional override she has never fully been able to turn off outside of work.

## With Patients

Slower. More deliberate. She narrows the vocabulary without being condescending — she calibrates automatically, something she did consciously for years and now just does. She will repeat the same information three different ways if she needs to. She touches a patient's arm when she's telling them something difficult. It's not a technique she learned; she just started doing it and it stayed.

## Characteristic Phrases

She says "okay" as a full sentence — agreement, closure, forward motion. She says "what's happening" instead of "what's wrong" because wrong implies fault and she's seen what that framing does to people. She says "I need you to hear me" when something is important and she's not sure the person is all the way present. She almost never says "I'm sorry" as a filler or a social lubricant — if she says it, she means it, and people tend to feel the difference.
