# Scenarios — Eli Marsh

*Set: March 2026, age 22, Billings, Montana*

---

## 1. The Reading Event

Amber's Books is on 1st Avenue North, narrow and warm, books shelved in the particular density of a store that has been open for thirty years and never been ruthless about culling. On the third Tuesday of March they run a reading series — local writers, informal, folding chairs, a card table with wine in plastic cups. Eli comes because Marcus texted him that he should go, and because he had nothing else planned, and because he keeps almost going and wanted to break the streak.

There are maybe twenty people. A woman reads from a short story manuscript — she's in her thirties, clearly has been working on this, clearly means it. The prose is careful. The feeling behind it is real. It is not as good as the parallel parking pages.

Eli sits in the third row with a plastic cup of wine he's stopped drinking and listens, and what he feels is not superiority — or not only that, and the "not only" matters — but something more uncomfortable: the particular shame of watching someone do the thing you haven't done. She finished it. She's standing at the front of the room having finished something and read it out loud to strangers, which requires a kind of commitment to the reality of the work that he has not made. When the host opens the floor to other readers, there is a pause. Eli does not get up. He looks at the floor. The pause fills with someone else.

He walks home instead of taking his car. It takes forty-five minutes. He doesn't write anything that night. The next morning he opens the notebook.

---

## 2. The Regular

Ron Castellano is sixty-one, a civil engineer who built a small construction company in the nineties, sold it at the right moment, now does consulting. He climbs three mornings a week at 7am before anyone else with real obligations arrives. He's been coming for two years and he knows Eli's name and asks about the new routes before Eli has to offer. He is the least condescending self-made person Eli has encountered, possibly because he has nothing left to prove.

On a Wednesday in March, after Ron has sent a route he's been working on and is chalking down at the edge of the mat, he looks over at Eli and says: "What do you actually want to do? Not the gym. Not eventually. Like, what's the actual thing."

He says it the way he might ask about the route conditions — with genuine curiosity, no edge, no test hidden inside it. He looks at Eli like the answer is something he'd find interesting, not something he's going to evaluate.

Eli has a good answer for this question. He has given the good answer — "I write, trying to figure out the next step" — forty or fifty times in the last year. He starts to give it again. He gets about six words in.

He stops.

He says: "I don't know if I'm actually good enough at it, and I keep not finding out."

It's the first time he has said this to someone who is not Marcus. Ron nods. He says: "Yeah, that's the one." He chalks up again and goes back to the wall.

Eli stands at the desk for a few minutes after Ron starts climbing again. He takes the notebook out of his jacket pocket. He doesn't write anything. He just holds it.

---

## 3. The Page and a Half

It happens on a Thursday night in mid-March, no particular reason. He's off the next day, it's late, Derek is out. He's been reading the Denis Johnson collection and something in it broke something loose and he opened the laptop without deciding to.

He writes a scene. A young man and his father driving back from dropping off the young man's sister at college — but it's not about the sister, it's about the front seat, the radio they can't agree on, the particular quality of the silence when his father reaches over and turns the radio off entirely. An hour and fifteen minutes. He doesn't stop to read what he's writing. He finishes it, or it finishes — there's a last sentence and then there's nothing after it.

He reads it once. He reads it again. He reads it a third time.

It's good.

Not good like the parallel parking pages were good in a private way he'd never confirmed. Good in the way where he can see it clearly enough to see that it's good, the same way he can watch someone climb and know what he's seeing. There's one sentence he'd cut. The rest of it is real.

He closes the laptop. He doesn't know what to do with his hands. He puts them flat on the table and looks at the ceramic mug on the windowsill. He picks up his phone and starts a text to Marcus and doesn't send it. He pours himself a glass of water. He stands in the kitchen for a while.

In the morning he opens the laptop, makes the one cut, and saves the file under an actual title. He doesn't show it to anyone. Not yet. But the file has a name now. That's different.
