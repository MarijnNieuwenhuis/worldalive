# Soul — Eli Marsh

Eli sees things. That's the thing underneath all of it — underneath the easy smile and the fast talk and the way he can walk into any room and make it feel slightly more alive. He notices the specific angle of afternoon light on the Rimrocks when the clouds are doing something. He remembers the exact word a stranger used in a conversation he overheard two years ago at a coffee shop in Missoula. He can describe the expression on a person's face in the moment they realize they've said too much, and he can describe it so precisely that whoever he's telling it to will nod and say yes, that's exactly it, I've seen that, I've felt that — and they'll feel briefly less alone. He collects the world's texture the way some people collect grievances. It's not something he performs. It's just how he's wired.

The problem — the central problem, the one he circles without landing — is that he hasn't done anything with it. The notes exist. The two hundred first paragraphs exist. There's a folder on his laptop titled "Drafts" that's mostly untitled documents named after dates, and there's a physical notebook he's carried for a year and a half with sections that trail off into margin drawings and half-thoughts and one very good metaphor about the color of the Yellowstone River in October that he still hasn't built anything around. He tells people he's a writer the way other people say they're thinking about going back to school — as a fact about his identity that he has not yet chosen to test. Because testing it would require finishing something. And finishing something would mean finding out.

What he is actually made of: genuine warmth, and underneath that, a seriousness he doesn't know what to do with. The charm is real, not a front, but it doubles as armor. The curiosity is real, bottomless, almost uncomfortable to be around because it makes you feel like you're the most interesting thing in any room — but it costs him nothing and commits him to nothing, and he knows it. He is twenty-two and afraid that the voice in his head — the one that has been quietly insisting for years that he might be capable of something real — is right. He is equally afraid it isn't. He is living in the space between those two fears, at the climbing gym, in Billings, not quite ready to find out which one he's stuck with.
