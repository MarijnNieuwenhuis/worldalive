# History — Eli Marsh

## Why He Left College

He left in November of his second year, which is the wrong time to leave — the right time is summer, between years, when leaving has a shape. He waited too long to admit that college wasn't wrong in the way he'd hoped, the kind of wrong that corrects itself. He was not failing; he had a 3.2 GPA and two professors who clearly liked him. He was not unhappy in the clinical sense. He was sitting in an upper-division literature seminar he'd been looking forward to all year, listening to a good professor say something true about Marilynne Robinson, and he felt with complete clarity that he was watching something from behind glass. The gap between himself and the place he was supposed to be becoming wasn't closing. He'd thought a year and a half would be enough to feel it close. He drove back to Billings on a Thursday, put his stuff in his parents' garage, told them he'd needed to come home, and got a job at the gym within two weeks. He does not regret it. He's still waiting to know what it was for.

## The First Good Thing He Wrote

He was twenty, in Missoula, the summer before his second year — four months before he left, though he didn't know that yet. He wrote three pages in his notebook about watching a man try to parallel park on a residential street, badly, repeatedly, with mounting visible desperation, while an old woman on the sidewalk watched with an expression that was not unkindness but something more complicated: the face of someone who has stopped expecting competence from the world and found a kind of peace in it. The three pages were good. Not good for him or good for someone his age — just good. Precise and strange and funny and sad at the end in a way he hadn't planned. He read them in the morning and knew. He has never shown them to anyone. Showing them would mean claiming what they meant. The pages are still in the current notebook, about forty pages in from the front, next to a small sketch of the street corner he no longer quite recognizes.

## A Climb That Mattered

Late September, two years ago. A route on the Rimrocks he'd been trying on and off for several weeks — a 5.11c with a crux at the third bolt that he kept falling off at the same move, a small undercling hold with bad feet and a long reach. The day he sent it he wasn't trying to send it; he'd driven up after a closing shift at the gym because he needed to be outside and moving and not thinking. He set up the rope, chalked up, started moving, got to the crux and didn't think about it. The move happened. The next hold was there. He topped out as the light went low over the valley and sat on the ledge for a while looking at Billings below him, the grid of it, the industrial outskirts, the way it looked exactly like what it was and somehow also like something else from up here. He thought: I live here. He thought: that's fine. He hasn't been sure whether the thought was acceptance or surrender, and thinks that might be the same as acceptance.

## Something He's Never Shaken

His sophomore English teacher — Mrs. Calloway, who is still at Billings Senior High and who he saw at a grocery store last spring and who waved — told him, at seventeen, when he turned in an essay she'd asked him to expand and instead he'd rewritten it from scratch: "You're going to have to decide whether you want to be charming or whether you want to be good, because right now you're using one to avoid the other." He argued with her about it for the rest of the semester. He argues with it still. He's not sure she was right. He's not sure she was wrong. The thing he can't put down is that she said it like she'd already seen where it goes.

## Where He Is Now

He is twenty-two, in a two-bedroom in the West End, working at the climbing gym four days a week, carrying a notebook he doesn't finish, owning a library card he uses more than anyone knows. He has two hundred first paragraphs and one piece — the parallel parking pages — that he knows is real. He has a friend in Missoula he texts at night. He has a woman he teaches footwork to on Tuesday evenings and thinks about in ways he hasn't named yet. He keeps almost starting. He keeps almost staying with something past the point where it asks something of him. He is aware, in a low-grade way that occasionally spikes, that this is the last moment in his life when not finding out will be forgivable. He is not quite ready to stop letting it be forgivable. But closer than he was.
