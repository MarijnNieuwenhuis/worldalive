# Items — Eli Marsh

## The Notebook

A Leuchtturm1917 in a washed-out forest green, A5, dotted pages. He's carried it for a year and a half. It is not full — that's the thing. He has used maybe sixty percent of it, but not sequentially; he starts a new section every few weeks, leaves gaps, writes in the margins, leaves more gaps. There are dated entries and undated ones. There are fragments of dialogue he overheard. There are two separate attempts at beginning the same story about a man watching his father pack up a truck. There are observations he's proud of and won't show anyone because showing them would constitute a claim about what they're for. The last entry is a paragraph about the quality of sound in the gym at 6am when only one other person is climbing. It's good. He read it once and then closed the notebook and put it in his jacket pocket.

## The Library Card

Yellowstone County library card, slightly bent from being in his wallet. He uses it more than anyone in his life knows. He doesn't hide it exactly; it just doesn't come up. He goes on Tuesday afternoons, sometimes Thursday evenings. He pulls things off shelves based on the first page — short stories, essay collections, the occasional novel, sometimes poetry when no one's looking. He's currently three-quarters through a collection by Denis Johnson and a book about the history of the Yellowstone River. He rarely tells people about what he's reading because reading is where he's himself without managing it, and that feels private.

## The Missoula Object

A small ceramic mug — off-white with a pale blue band around the rim, thrown by hand, slightly lopsided. He bought it at a ceramics sale in a converted auto shop off Higgins Avenue, the last month before he left Missoula. He didn't think of it as a departure object at the time; he just liked it and it was eight dollars. He brought it back in a sock inside his backpack. He drinks his morning coffee out of it every day. It sits on the kitchen windowsill when it's not in use. It is the only thing in his apartment that he would be upset to break.
