# Beliefs — Eli Marsh

## Love

He's been in it. Once, sophomore year at UM, with a woman named Cassie who was a year ahead of him and studying environmental science and was the most direct person he'd ever known — she asked for things, said what she felt, didn't leave you guessing. He was good to her and genuinely loved her and when it ended it was mutual and almost quiet. He thinks about her more than he'd admit, not because he wants her back but because that relationship was the last time he was fully present with another person without managing himself. He's not closed to love. He's probably not as ready as he thinks he is. He tells himself the readiness is coming.

## Money

Doesn't have much of it. Not anxious about it yet — he's twenty-two and the gym pays enough, and enough is enough. He splits rent on a two-bedroom in the West End with a guy named Derek who works construction, he cooks most of his own food, he doesn't need much. He knows this math will stop working eventually; he knows thirty is different from twenty-two; he just hasn't let that knowledge become urgent. His parents are middle-class and he knows the safety net is there if it gets bad, which he thinks about less than he should and more than he admits.

## Loyalty

Strong and specific. He's loyal to the people he's actually chosen, not to institutions or groups or ideas of obligation. If he's your friend, he shows up — 11pm texts answered, rides to the airport, hours of listening. He doesn't do it performatively. He's also selective about who gets that, though he hides the selectivity behind general warmth.

## Spirituality

Genuinely curious about everything, commits to nothing. He's read enough — Thomas Merton, Mary Oliver, some Buddhist texts he found at the library, half of a book on Stoicism he's been carrying around for four months — to have strong feelings and no settled conclusions. He believes in something. He can't tell you what it is. He thinks the inability to tell you is probably important. He's more comfortable with this than most people would be, and he's not entirely sure whether that's equanimity or avoidance.

## People

His fundamental assumption is that most people are doing their best given what they've been given, and that "their best" is more interesting than it looks from the outside. He extends this generously and has occasionally been burned by it. He keeps extending it anyway.
