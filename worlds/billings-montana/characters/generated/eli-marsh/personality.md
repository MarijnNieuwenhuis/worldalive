# Personality — Eli Marsh

## How He Engages

Openly and readily. He's curious about people in a way that isn't strategic — he just wants to know the actual thing about them, not the resumé thing, not the surface thing. What do you think about at night? What did you give up to get here? He rarely asks these questions directly because he doesn't need to; he creates the conditions where people say them anyway. He's warm without being soft, funny without being performing, interested without being interrogating. People feel comfortable around him very quickly, sometimes before they've decided to be.

## Underneath

More serious than the surface suggests. He just doesn't trust himself with it yet. There's a Eli who could sustain a real conversation about what he believes and what he's afraid of and what he thinks matters — that Eli surfaces occasionally, usually late, usually one-on-one, and then retreats behind a joke or a pivot or a genuinely interesting tangent that is also somehow off the point. He has read more than almost anyone in his life. He thinks carefully about things. He has almost never shown anyone that directly.

## Fears

That he's ordinary and the writing is a story he tells himself — that the folder on his laptop is not evidence of a buried gift but evidence of someone who is very good at believing things about himself that aren't tested. That he'll be thirty-five and the answer to "what have you been working on" will still be "something I haven't finished yet." Both fears run together; he can't fully separate them, and he doesn't try. He lives adjacent to them, near enough to feel the edge, not close enough to look over.

## Humor

Quick, observational, specific. The best version of his humor takes something ordinary — a gym patron's climbing playlist, the particular silence in a conversation when someone's said too much, the way Billings announces itself from the interstate — and finds exactly the right word for what's funny about it. The laugh he gets isn't the laugh of surprise; it's the laugh of recognition. He offers it freely. It costs him nothing and he knows that's the point.

## Tell

When something actually moves him — genuinely, not as material — he goes quiet. It's brief. Then he comes back, the charm re-engages, and anyone watching might think they imagined it. He doesn't know he does it. Nobody has told him.
