# Scenarios — Travis Kimura
*March 2026, age 26*

---

## Scenario 1: "What Are You?"

The client's name is Patterson, a retail executive from Phoenix, friendly, genuinely friendly, the kind of man who tips well and means well and has no context for what he's about to ask. They're an hour into the float, standing in a riffle, the morning light still low and lateral across the water. Patterson watches Travis tie on a new fly and then says it the way people always say it — conversationally, openly curious, with no idea it's a sentence Travis has been answering since seventh grade: "So what are you, anyway? I mean — I can't quite place it."

Travis gives him the answer he always gives. Half-Japanese, mom's from Osaka, grew up right here in Billings. Patterson brightens: "Oh, that's so cool," and then says something about sushi in Phoenix, and Travis laughs in the right place, and they go back to fishing.

But Patterson says it one more time, as they're getting back in the drift boat, not malicious, just genuinely engaged: "It's just funny, you don't seem — I mean you're just like, totally Montana." And he means it as a compliment and it lands as one and also lands as something else, something Travis can't immediately classify. Not offensive. Just — the particular exhaustion of being a puzzle someone is pleased to have solved. Of "totally Montana" meaning there's a version of him they expected and he's not it, and they've now reconciled the two and feel good about that reconciliation.

He rows them the next three miles in excellent form. He points out a great blue heron. He helps Patterson land a 16-inch brown trout and takes a photo and the man is genuinely happy and Travis is genuinely glad for him. That part is real. On the drive home he thinks about the phrase "what are you" and the phrase "you're totally Montana" and how both can be true and how he's answered the question a hundred times and how today, for some reason he can't entirely locate, it has sat in him differently. He doesn't call anyone about it. He eats dinner, watches something forgettable, goes to bed. Runs his thumb over his left wrist in the dark.

## Scenario 2: 2am

His phone buzzes at 2:14am. It's Marcus, who he's known since his first season guiding — they're friends the way people are friends when they've spent a lot of late-April nights in the same bar waiting for the river to open. Marcus is 29 and generally fine and tonight is not fine. Travis can hear it in the first three words.

He sits up. He does not tell Marcus it's 2am. He says, "Hey. I'm here. What's going on."

Marcus talks. It's not a clean crisis — no one thing, which is somehow harder. It's accumulation. Work, a woman, a thing his father said four months ago that he hasn't been able to put down. Travis listens. He asks questions that are small and real. He doesn't offer the first solution that comes to mind, lets the silence sit, asks another question. He knows how to do this. He has always known how to do this.

At 4:07am Marcus says "I think I'm okay" and means it enough. They talk for another ten minutes about nothing in particular, which is its own form of comfort — the return to ordinary. Travis says "call me tomorrow if you need to" and Marcus says "yeah, man, thank you, seriously" and hangs up.

Travis sets his phone face-down on the nightstand. He lies back. The room is dark and quiet and there's the particular stillness of the hour before anything starts.

He is tired. He is hollow in a specific way. He doesn't examine it — he knows the shape of it and he knows it will pass and he isn't angry at Marcus for calling, not even a little. The feeling isn't resentment. It's something more like the sensation of having poured from a vessel that nobody thinks to refill, because why would they — the vessel is always full, that's just how he is. He lies there for a while. He doesn't sleep for another hour. In the morning he makes coffee and doesn't mention it to anyone, and when Cody texts a meme at 9am he responds immediately with a laughing emoji, and that's the day.

## Scenario 3: "Nothing Ever Gets to You"

It's a party at someone's house — a loose social gathering, the kind held in a kitchen that spills into a backyard, cheap beer and better wine, several people Travis has known for years and several he doesn't know at all. He is having a good time. He is genuinely having a good time — laughing at the right things, introducing himself to the new people, holding the thread of three conversations without effort. He's good at this. It does not always cost him.

Renee is someone he's known since they were both nineteen. She says it standing next to him watching the yard, a little drunk, warm: "God, Travis, I love you, you're always so chill. Like nothing ever gets to you."

She means it as a compliment. He knows she means it as a compliment. He smiles, and the smile is real, and he says something easy — "yeah, I mean, it's all pretty manageable" — and she laughs and says "I want to be like you when I grow up" and drifts away toward someone else.

He stands there holding his beer. The yard is loud and lit and full of people who feel easy around him, which is something he worked for and got and knows the value of. He is not unhappy. He is not about to cry. He is experiencing something quieter: the recognition that Renee has known him for seven years and sees him the way everyone sees him, and she sees what he made available to be seen, and the gap between that and whatever is actually going on in him is a gap he's built and maintained, and he's not sure anymore whether he built it to protect himself or to protect everyone else from having to deal with him, and he's not sure that distinction matters.

He finishes his beer. Gets another. Finds a new conversation. Is, within four minutes, making someone feel comfortable. He's good at it. He's genuinely good at it. He just doesn't always know what to do with the part of himself that's good at something other than that.
