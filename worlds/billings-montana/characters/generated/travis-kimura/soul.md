# Soul — Travis Kimura

Travis wants people to feel okay. Not in a surface-level, socially-trained way — he genuinely does not like watching people struggle alone, does not like the particular silence that settles over a room when something's wrong and no one's saying it. He moves toward that. He fills it. He learned young that he was good at it, and by the time he could have asked whether he wanted to keep doing it, it had already become who he was. The want is real. The cost is quieter.

The cost is that he's spent so long monitoring other people's comfort that his own has become something he forgets to check on. He'll go three weeks without noticing he's tired. He'll stay on a phone call for two hours with someone in crisis and feel, afterward, not resentful — never resentful — but hollowed out in a way he doesn't have a word for. He's not a martyr about it. He doesn't even fully recognize it as a pattern. He just knows that sometimes he looks up from a day and can't locate himself in it.

The river is the exception. The Yellowstone doesn't need him to be anything — not calm, not warm, not easy. He can stand in moving water and just be a body in current, thinking about drag and drift and what the fish are doing underneath, and there's no performance required. He's told clients the river is meditative, which is true but only part of it. The fuller truth is that it's the one place he doesn't have to hold anything together. What he holds onto when he's not performing ease: the memory of his grandmother's hands on a bowl of water, the way she moved through her own language as though it were a place. A fly he tied himself that he's never cast. The fact that cold river water on his wrists, first thing in the morning, feels like arriving somewhere real.
