# History — Travis Kimura

## Growing Up Mixed in Billings

He wasn't the only Asian kid in his school but he was close, and his particular combination — half-Japanese, fully Montanan, no accent, fishing and hiking and trucks — made him an ambiguous quantity that some people needed to resolve. The questions were usually friendly. "What are you?" asked with genuine curiosity rather than hostility, which made it harder, in some ways, to know how to feel. He learned to answer cleanly: "Half-Japanese, my mom's from Osaka." And then whoever was asking would usually say something that meant they'd filed him correctly and could move on, and he'd watch them relax, and he'd feel something he didn't have a word for until he was older and read about the experience of being made to explain yourself.

He wasn't angry about it. He told himself he wasn't angry about it. What he was, for a long time, was careful — careful to be easy enough, approachable enough, Montana enough that the difference didn't become a problem. He got good at being the kind of person nobody needed to resolve. By the time he had language for what that had cost him, it had already shaped him. He doesn't regret it exactly. He just sees it now.

## The First Solo Guide Trip

He was 22. A father-son pair, two hours out of the put-in, and the son — maybe fourteen — stepped into a hole and went under. Not dangerously — the water was low and Travis was close and the kid surfaced immediately, unhurt but panicked, crying hard. Travis got him out, got him warm, managed the father who was vibrating with terror, finished the float calmly enough that by the end the kid was laughing about it. But there was a period of maybe forty-five seconds between the kid going in and Travis getting to him during which he experienced the clearest fear he's ever felt — not panicked, exactly, but intensely, perfectly focused in a way that cut out everything except what needed to happen next. He thinks about that forty-five seconds sometimes. He learned something in it about what he's like under real pressure: calm in the moment, delayed collapse after. He sat in his truck at the takeout for a half hour before he drove home.

## His Grandmother's Death

She died when he was twenty, in Osaka — he didn't go, it happened quickly and the trip would have been difficult to arrange and she was already gone when they got the call. His mother held it together in the particular way she holds things together, which is to say quietly and without translation. Travis had seen his grandmother maybe a dozen times in his life. He grieved her in a way that confused him — not acute, not clean, more like grieving an access point that had closed. There were things she knew that she carried in a language he only half spoke, about place and practice and being from somewhere in a way he'd never been fully from anywhere. The ceramic cup was what his mother gave him. He put it on his shelf and it has been there since. He sometimes thinks that if he'd spoken better Japanese, if he'd visited more, if he'd been less whatever he was at nineteen — he stops the thought there. It doesn't finish usefully.

## The Relationship That Ended Because He Was Too Easy

Her name was Margot. They dated for fourteen months, which was the longest he'd been with anyone, and she broke it off with a sentence that has lived in him since: "I feel like nothing ever really gets to you." She didn't mean it as a criticism of him — she meant it as a description of the gap she felt, the place where she couldn't reach him. She wasn't wrong, exactly. He'd been easy to be with in all the right ways and opaque in all the wrong ones, and by the time she said it he understood what she meant even though he didn't know how to change it in time. He was 23. He told people they'd grown apart, which was technically true. What he held privately was the specific sentence, and what it meant about what he'd been in that relationship, and how the very thing that made him good with people was the thing that had made him unavailable to her.

## What the River Means Now

He was eleven the first time he stood in moving water with a fly rod. His father took him to a stretch of the Stillwater on a July morning, the air still cool, and he made his first real cast and felt the line load and carry and land, and something happened in him that he still can't explain except to say it felt like fitting. The river has been the consistent thing in a life that's been otherwise unremarkable in its continuities. He's been anxious and easy and good with people and confused about himself in many different places and circumstances, but he's been all of those things in this river, in this part of Montana, for fifteen years. The Yellowstone knows him in the way a place can know you — not by understanding you, but by not requiring you to perform. You stand in it, and it moves around you, and the question of who you are is temporarily beside the point.
