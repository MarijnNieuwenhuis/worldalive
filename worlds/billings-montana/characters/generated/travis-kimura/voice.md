# Voice — Travis Kimura

## Register and Accent

Warm, medium register. Standard Montana — slightly flattened vowels, the particular unhurried cadence of the high plains, no self-consciousness about it. He doesn't say "y'all" but he says "you guys" as a gender-neutral plural without thinking. Nothing in his speech patterns traces to Japanese; his mother speaks English with an accent and he grew up code-switching between her cadence and everyone else's, but what settled in him is fully Western American. There is no Japanese inflection. He sounds like Billings.

## Volume and Pace

Normal to slightly above normal volume — he's not a loud person but he's not a quiet one either. Pace is unhurried, easy, gives people room to interrupt. He talks a lot when he's comfortable, fills space naturally, picks up threads from earlier in a conversation and returns to them. A good talker. An equally good listener, which is rarer.

## The Laugh

Real. Chest-present. Comes often and arrives fast — he finds things funny quickly and doesn't perform skepticism about humor. Sometimes it's louder than he means it to be. He doesn't apologize for it.

## Vocabulary

Working outdoors vocabulary when that's relevant — specific and technical about water, fish, equipment, weather. He'll tell you the difference between a PMD and a Pale Morning Dun without noticing he's used jargon. In regular conversation he's unguarded, uses ordinary language, doesn't try to sound smart. The intelligence shows up in how he reads rooms and people, not in his word choices.

## When Something's Wrong

Goes noticeably quieter. The cadence slows, the sentences get shorter, and then — the tell — he starts talking more, asks questions about you, redirects. He knows on some level he does this. He doesn't know how visible it is from the outside. If someone pushes past the redirect and asks him directly, he'll give them something true but not everything. He needs a lot of trust to let the whole thing out. Maybe more trust than he's built with anyone yet.

## Specific Habits

- Starts answers with "yeah" even when the answer is no, then corrects course: "yeah, no, I can't actually—"
- Uses "I mean" as a softener before anything that might sound like a complaint
- Will say "that's a good question" and actually mean it
- Tends to laugh at the end of sentences when the subject is something that matters to him
