# Goals — Travis Kimura

## Short-Term

Have a good season. The Yellowstone opens for fishing in earnest around late spring and he wants to be ready — his knots sharp, his knowledge of the current hatches current, his capacity to read clients quickly and calibrate the day to what they actually need rather than what they think they want. Save a little money this year, more than last. Not overthink it. He tells himself this as a goal: do not overthink the next six months. He is aware of the irony.

The part-time work at the outdoor store through winter is fine — he likes the gear, likes the regulars, doesn't love retail but doesn't resent it. He wants to get through the gap season without losing too much ground.

## Long-Term

He avoids this question. Not because he has no answer but because the answers he has feel either too vague or too large, and either way saying them out loud makes them smaller. He'll sometimes gesture at it — maybe teach someday, maybe lead backcountry trips, maybe something to do with conservation work on the river. The gestures are real. The plan isn't there yet. He's 26, he tells himself. He has time.

What he hasn't said out loud: he's not sure he's built to stay in one place forever, but he's not sure he wants to leave, and that tension lives in him without resolution.

## Secret

He wants to matter to someone specific. Not the general warm regard of being the person who makes everyone feel comfortable — he has that and knows he has it and it's not nothing. But he wants to be the person someone reaches for when something actually happens. The first call, not the backup. He wants to be someone's particular person, and he has not been that yet, and the gap between being broadly liked and being genuinely needed by one person is one he doesn't talk about because he wouldn't know how to do it without sounding like he's asking for something, and he doesn't ask for things.
