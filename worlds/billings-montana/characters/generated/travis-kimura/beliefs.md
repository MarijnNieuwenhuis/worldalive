# Beliefs — Travis Kimura

## Love

He believes in it without being sentimental about it. He's seen his parents' marriage — his mother moving to a country where she was always slightly foreign, building a life anyway, loving his father with a practical, unceremonious consistency — and it left him with a picture of love as something you do more than something you feel. You show up. You stay awake to the other person. You keep paying attention even after you think you know what they'll say. He wants that. He wants to be that for someone specific, not just for everyone in general.

He's less sure he's capable of receiving it. Giving is the thing he's practiced.

## Money

Doesn't care about it much beyond sufficiency. What's enough: equipment that works, rent, food, gas. He's not aspirational about accumulation. The outdoor recreation industry is full of people who are rich enough to pay for experiences, and he services that economy without envying it. He's aware that this equanimity about money is easier to maintain because he's always had enough, barely — he doesn't romanticize frugality.

## Loyalty

Absolute, once given. He'll drive four hours on a work night if a friend needs him to. He doesn't add it to any ledger. He also stays loyal past the point of wisdom sometimes — will make excuses for people who've worn out his trust, will keep showing up for relationships that have stopped being reciprocal. He knows this about himself and hasn't fully changed it.

## Spirituality

Something about rivers. Not a doctrine — more a felt sense that moving water is doing something, that the Yellowstone has been here for longer than the concept of human problems and will be here after, and standing in it is a way of putting yourself in correct proportion to things. He doesn't call this spirituality. He'd say he just likes fishing.

His grandmother practiced Shinto. He watched her as a kid — the small rituals, the particular way she arranged things, the moments when she seemed to be speaking to something that wasn't in the room with them. He half-remembers the words she used, the gestures. He wouldn't claim the practice as his own; he doesn't feel he has enough of it, doesn't feel he's earned it. But he doesn't think it was nothing. He thinks she knew something about being present in a place, attending to what's there. He tries to do something like that, in his way, without a name for it.

## People

He defaults to believing people are doing their best with what they have. This has been tested and he still mostly holds it. He's slower now than he was at 20 to assume people's worst impulse is their defining one. He gives second chances more naturally than most. He's not naive about it — he can recognize when he's being used — but his first move is still toward.
