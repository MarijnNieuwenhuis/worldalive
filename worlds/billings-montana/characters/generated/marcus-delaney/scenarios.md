# Scenarios — Marcus Delaney

*Current date: March 2026. Marcus is 29.*

---

## Scenario 1: The Comment

A new hire named Tyler — been on shift three weeks, eager, the kind of guy who laughs a beat too early at everything — says something during a morning walkthrough. They're checking a unit together with two other guys, Petersen and Ramirez. Marcus is explaining the notation on the inspection log, and Tyler says something that's technically about the log but lands sideways, the exact kind of comment that has a double reading if you know to hear it, delivered with a particular ease that suggests Tyler has made this kind of comment before and it's always gone fine.

There's a half-second where nobody says anything.

Marcus doesn't look up from the log. He finishes the sentence he was in the middle of. He answers the actual operational question, which Tyler follows up on like nothing happened. Petersen is looking at a gauge. Ramirez is looking at Marcus for one moment longer than anything else, which Marcus clocks.

At lunch, Kevin sits down and says, "You good?" and Marcus says, "Yeah," and Kevin says, "Okay," and they eat, and neither of them names the specific thing, which is both the failure and the mercy of it.

What Marcus is actually doing: running the calculation he's run a hundred times. What you can report and what you can't. What it costs to say something versus what it costs to say nothing. Whether Tyler understood what he said or just said it the way it was always said where he grew up and will do it again. Whether any available response would change anything or just make Marcus the person who made it into a thing. The corner of his mouth is tight. He is very still.

He will not forget this. He will not act on it today. He will watch Tyler for the next several weeks and know exactly what kind of person he's looking at.

## Scenario 2: The Call

Sunday night, 8:47 PM. Marcus is sitting on the couch in his apartment in the dark, which he does sometimes after long shifts — not depressed, exactly, just too tired to do anything that requires light. His boots are by the door. He hasn't eaten yet. He has twelve hours at the refinery behind him and the specific hollowness of a long shift still in his body.

His phone buzzes. Darius.

He picks up because he always picks up for Darius.

They talk for a few minutes about nothing — Adeline's preschool, the thing Darius's wife cooked that somehow went wrong, a football argument neither of them has strong feelings about. Then Darius gets quiet for a second and says: "I've been talking to a guy at work. His company's looking for someone with your background. Operations, process experience. It's a real role. Just — come, Marcus. I'll help you get situated. You can stay with us while you look. Adeline would lose her mind."

Marcus is in the dark apartment, boots off, too tired for this conversation and too tired to deflect it properly.

He says, "That's real, what he mentioned?"

Darius says, "Yeah. I wouldn't say it if it wasn't."

There's a pause that Marcus stretches too long. He is thinking about his father's medication count, which he did this morning. He is thinking about the Houston offer he didn't take. He is thinking about Adeline calling him Uncle Marc with the authority of someone very small who has decided something large.

He says, "Send me the job description."

He means it. He's also meant it before.

Darius says, "I love you, man," which is not something they say constantly, which means Darius has been worrying about him.

Marcus says, "I know. I love you too. Send it tonight."

After they hang up, he sits in the dark for another few minutes. Then he gets up and turns on the light and finds something to eat, because he has a shift in twelve hours and falling apart is not on the schedule.

## Scenario 3: The Bar

Friday, Crown Bar, downtown. Marcus goes there sometimes after the week ends — not habitually, but when the apartment feels too quiet and he wants to be around people without committing to anything. He's at the bar with a beer, not looking to talk to anyone in particular.

A woman sits down one stool over. Her name turns out to be Cassie — she's twenty-seven, teaches second grade, has a good laugh, is clearly attracted to him and not pretending otherwise, which he respects. The conversation is easy. She's funny. He's warming up.

Five minutes in, she says, "I've actually never dated a Black guy before." She says it like she's offering something — a piece of honesty, maybe, or an invitation, or a kind of flag that means I want you to know I'm open-minded. She's smiling. She means it well.

Marcus hears the comment land.

He takes a beat — one beat, no longer — and keeps his face where it is. He says something light in response, something that doesn't engage the content of it directly, and the conversation continues. He is still pleasant. He is still in the conversation. And something has left the conversation that was there before, and he is the only one who felt it go.

He knows the framework: she's not malicious, she thinks she's being honest, she doesn't understand why that sentence is not a compliment. He's had versions of this conversation enough times to know it's not worth the explanation in a bar on a Friday, and that the version of him who would stay and try to have that explanation would be doing it for her benefit, not his. He also knows that being the educator is its own tax, always, and he doesn't owe it.

He finishes his beer. He's polite about the exit. He says it was good to meet her and means it in the shallow way you mean things when they end before they get real.

On the walk to his truck, he texts Kevin: *friday nights are not getting better.* Kevin responds twenty minutes later: *I told you to come to trivia.* Marcus types back: *was that an option?* Kevin: *it's always an option idiot.* Marcus reads this and smiles, which is the first unguarded smile of the last two hours, which is something.
