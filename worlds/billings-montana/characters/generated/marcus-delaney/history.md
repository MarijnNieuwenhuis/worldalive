# History — Marcus Delaney

## Growing Up the Only One

His parents moved to Billings from Memphis when Marcus was three and Darius was six — Raymond Delaney had a job offer, and it was 1999, and they went. The neighborhood was fine. The neighbors were mostly fine. The schools were where the particular texture of it started.

Marcus was the only Black kid in most of his classes from first grade through high school, with a few exceptions. He learned early — too early, before he had language for it — how to exist in a room in a way that didn't make anyone uncomfortable. How to be smart without being threatening about it. How to be one of the guys without quite being one of the guys. He was liked, genuinely — he wasn't miserable, he had friends, he had a good high school experience by most measures. But there was always a register underneath it, a low hum of adjustment and awareness that the other kids didn't have to run. He didn't think of it as unfair for a long time. It was just the conditions.

He went to Montana State Billings for two years before he ran out of money and ran out of direction. He's thought about finishing his degree. He's thought about it several times.

## Getting the Refinery Job

He was twenty-four and working retail and it was getting old. Kevin's brother-in-law was a foreman at the CHS refinery and mentioned they were hiring. Marcus had no background in process work, but he was methodical and physically capable and willing to start at the bottom, and the foreman hired him. The pay was better than anything he'd had. The benefits were real. He told himself it was temporary — a year, maybe two, save some money, figure out the next thing.

The next thing didn't clarify itself. He got good at the job. He got respected at the job. He is now one of the people the foreman points to when a new hire needs to shadow someone who does it right. He's not sure whether to be proud of this or to think harder about what it means that he stayed long enough to become that person.

## The Incident He Hasn't Forgotten

February 2023, an equipment check during a cold snap. There was a temporary worker — a contract hire, early twenties, from somewhere outside Billings. They were going over a valve sequence, and the guy made a comment: nothing he could be fired for, nothing that would hold up as anything formal, just the kind of casual small thing that was clearly something. The way he said it, the slight smile after, the glance at the other guy on the crew to see if it landed.

Marcus kept his face still and walked the guy through the rest of the valve sequence correctly and wrote up the check correctly and went home and sat with it. He didn't report it. He didn't say anything to Kevin. He's turned it over enough times to know why the guy did it and why it worked the way it was designed to work: you can't quite call it out without seeming like you're being sensitive, and either way you're managing it instead of doing your job. The contract hire didn't come back after the month was up. Marcus is still there. He knows which of those facts is supposed to be the win. Some days it doesn't feel like one.

## The Year He Almost Left

Spring 2024. He had an offer in Houston — operations coordinator at a refinery there, more money, actual advancement path, a city with real size and real diversity. He got through the whole process. He held the offer letter in his hand. He told himself he was going to take it.

He didn't take it. He said it was the relocation logistics, the timing, that he wanted to wait for something better. His brother knew it wasn't that, and said so, once, carefully, and Marcus said he'd think about it, and then they talked about Adeline's upcoming birthday and moved on. The Houston offer is the thing he thinks about on bad days at work: that he chose this, when he had a clear exit. He doesn't know what that means about him, exactly, and the not knowing is worse than whatever the answer might be.

## What's Keeping Him One More Year

His father's health is not great. Raymond Delaney is sixty-one, has a bad knee from the construction years and blood pressure that's been a problem for two years. Nothing catastrophic yet. But Marcus is in Billings, and his father is in Billings, and his mother goes to the doctor appointments but can't always follow the instructions home, and Marcus stops by on Sundays and checks the medication count and replaces the ones running low and doesn't tell his father he's doing this because Raymond Delaney is a man who does not like to need things.

He has not told Darius the full picture of their father's health, because Darius has a family and a life in Atlanta and worrying him won't help anything. He is managing this quietly, the way he manages most things. It is also, he knows, a reason to stay that is too good — too clean, too obviously right — to examine too closely for what else might be in it.
