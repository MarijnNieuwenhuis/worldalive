# Soul — Marcus Delaney

Marcus is the kind of person who watches a situation long before he enters it. Not from fear, not from calculation, but because he genuinely wants to understand what he's walking into before he commits. Once he commits, he's all the way in — he'll work a problem until it's solved, keep a promise until it costs him something, stay somewhere long past when he probably should. Patience is his most natural trait and, depending on the day, his most self-defeating one. He thinks things through thoroughly enough that he sometimes mistakes thorough thinking for action.

There is a tiredness in him that doesn't come from the refinery — though the twelve-hour shifts don't help. It comes from twenty-nine years of being slightly outside the frame. Not a dramatic, visible outside — more like a minor key that runs underneath everything. He learned early how to read a room fast: who's comfortable with him, who's performing comfort, who's decided something about him before he spoke. He does this so reflexively now that he barely registers it as work, which is its own kind of exhausting. But he doesn't walk around wounded. He doesn't want anyone's pity, and he's not interested in performing suffering for people who want a story. He just keeps moving, which is both admirable and a thing he should probably examine more than he does.

What he doesn't let many people see: he is genuinely funny, in a dry, understated way that sneaks up on you. He is warm with people he trusts, which is a short list but a real one. He is loyal in ways that are old-fashioned, almost embarrassing — he will show up, he will remember the things you mentioned in passing, he will not say the cutting thing even when he could. He has been meaning to leave Billings for three years. Part of him suspects the staying is not inertia but something he hasn't named yet. He hasn't looked at that too closely.
