# Items — Marcus Delaney

## The Photo

A 4x6 print, kept on the shelf above his TV rather than stored away. Him and his brother Darius at a family reunion in Atlanta, summer of 2023 — they're both wearing the same style of basketball shorts by coincidence, both laughing at something off-camera, both mid-motion. Marcus is maybe two inches taller; Darius has more of their mother's features. The photo captures something rare: Marcus looks completely unguarded. No reading-the-room, no slight vigilance, just laughing.

He doesn't look at it every day. He looks at it on the days when Billings feels very far from anywhere.

## The Headphones

Sony WH-1000XM5s. Over-ear, noise-canceling, bought with intention after three months of deliberation. He uses them on the commute to work when he drives with the radio off, in the apartment when he needs to not hear the building around him, on long evenings when what he needs is to be inside music for an hour. His playlist runs wide — he doesn't have a genre commitment, he has moods. D'Angelo for late nights. Kendrick when he needs to think hard about something. Occasionally, and privately, country. There are a few songs he's played a hundred times. He doesn't explain his listening to anyone.

The headphones are his private space made physical. When they're on, he is not available.

## His Father's Tape Measure

A Stanley 25-foot tape measure that belonged to his father, Raymond Delaney, who worked construction for twenty years before his knees gave out. Marcus doesn't do construction — he can do basic household repairs but nothing more — and doesn't need a 25-foot tape measure for anything in a refinery. He keeps it anyway, in the kitchen drawer with the other tools. The case is worn through to the metal at one corner. His father used it to measure everything: rooms, lumber, the height of Marcus and Darius on a wall in their childhood kitchen that neither of them lives near anymore.

He takes it out occasionally, not to use it. Just to have it in his hand for a minute. His father is still alive and still in Billings, and Marcus sees him on Sundays sometimes, and none of that makes the tape measure less important.
