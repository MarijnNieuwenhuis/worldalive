# Skills — Marcus Delaney

## Strong At

**Process work.** He understands systems — how refinery operations flow, where the failure points are, what a change in one part of the process means for everything downstream. He came in knowing nothing and learned by paying close attention for three years. He's good at following procedure and better at knowing when the procedure has a gap.

**Staying calm under pressure.** Not a performance of calm — actual calm, or something close enough to it that it functions the same way. In an emergency at work, his heart rate goes up and his voice goes down. He's been in two situations where equipment failed in ways that could have gone very badly. Both times he was the person other people looked to. Both times he knew what to do, or found out fast.

**Reading a room.** He does this instinctively and has done it for so long that he barely registers it consciously anymore. Who's comfortable, who's performing, where the tension is, what the subtext is. He's rarely wrong. This is useful professionally. It is occasionally exhausting personally.

**Reliability.** Not glamorous, but real. If he says he'll do something, it gets done. If he's going to be somewhere, he's there. This is rarer than it should be, and people who know him have noticed.

## Learning

Letting himself want things out loud. He's working on this, imperfectly. He's been practicing by telling his brother about the cities he's looked at, which felt enormous and was only slightly embarrassing. Sitting with uncertainty without immediately trying to resolve it. That one's harder.

## Bad At

Asking for help. Not because of pride, exactly — more because he's been the competent one for long enough that it's become a form of armor. The thought of needing something from someone, being in the position of waiting for them to give it, makes him uncomfortable in a way he can't fully articulate.

Leaving, apparently. He is aware of the irony.

## Hides

He has been applying to jobs in other cities for a year. He has three saved searches on Indeed and LinkedIn. He has gotten to the final interview stage twice — once in Denver, once in Houston — and both times found a reason it wasn't quite right. He has not told Kevin. He has not told his brother about the final interviews. He tells himself it's because he doesn't want to announce something until it's real. He's not entirely sure that's the reason.
