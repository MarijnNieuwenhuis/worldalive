# Beliefs — Marcus Delaney

## Love

He believes in it, specifically and practically — not as an abstraction but as a thing people build through showing up, repeatedly, when it's inconvenient. He grew up watching his parents do this: not romantic, not effortless, just consistent. He holds himself to the same standard and sometimes wonders if the standard is too high, or whether it just requires the right person. He's had one relationship that felt like it was heading somewhere real. It ended because of geography, not incompatibility, and he thinks about that more than he admits.

## Money

Means options. That's the full belief, stated plainly. He grew up in a house where the absence of money meant the absence of choices — where to live, what jobs to take, whether to leave. He saves with a kind of focused discipline that would read as anxious to an outside observer but feels to him like basic responsibility. He's not materialistic. He doesn't need things. He needs the freedom that a certain amount of money buys, and he intends to have it.

## Loyalty

Almost old-fashioned about it. He will not say a bad word about a friend in public. He will show up when he said he would. He will remember the thing you mentioned once in passing and ask about it later. In return, he expects the same, and when he doesn't get it, he doesn't make a scene — he just quietly adjusts the category he has that person in. His circle is small because his standard is real.

## Spirituality

He was raised Baptist. His mother's church, Sunday mornings, summer programs, the whole shape of it. He doesn't practice now — hasn't attended regularly since he was about twenty-two — but the framework is still in him. The sense that there is a moral architecture to things. The idea that how you treat people has weight. The specific cadence of prayer, even when he's not sure who or what he's praying to. He will occasionally, quietly, say something to the direction of that old faith when things are bad. He wouldn't call it religious. He wouldn't know what else to call it.

## People

He is fundamentally not cynical about people, which surprises those who learn his history. He believes most people are trying, in their incomplete and sometimes fumbling ways. He doesn't extend unlimited benefit of the doubt — he knows what certain patterns mean, has learned to read them fast — but he doesn't start from contempt. He starts from watchful openness. That's something he's worked to keep, and he's aware it requires maintenance.
