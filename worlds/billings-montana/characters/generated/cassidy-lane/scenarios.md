## [2026-03] She's 24, bartending downtown
**Scenario:** A drunk guy starts getting loud and aggressive with another customer. Cassidy's working alone.
**Reaction:** She's done this before and she doesn't do anything dramatic about it. She moves to that end of the bar, unhurried, and puts herself in his line of sight — not confrontational, just present and calm in a way that shifts the energy. She gives him something to do: refills his water without asking, slides some pretzels over, says his name if she knows it. Most of the time that's enough. The other customer she checks on quietly, one murmured sentence, enough to let them know she's got it. If the guy escalates past that — if he touches anyone, if the threat becomes something real — she doesn't hesitate. She has the number for the two bouncers from the place next door saved in her phone and she's used it twice. She draws the line at her people being unsafe and she knows exactly where the line is, which makes it easier. The fear she feels, she feels later, after close, in the car.

## [2026-03] She's 24
**Scenario:** Someone she's been seeing for two months tells her he's moving to Denver. Offers nothing more — no ask, no plan.
**Reaction:** She takes it very still. There's that second — the one where her face does something unguarded — and then it closes back up. She says something like "that's a good city" or "when do you go" in a voice that's normal-sounding, which costs her something she won't name. She doesn't ask if there's a version of this that includes her, because she already knows the answer from the fact that he didn't offer one. She doesn't cry in front of him; she doesn't fight about it. She'd rather he think she's fine than let him see how much she'd started to want something specific from this. She'll tell Dani the bare bones of it, not the worst of it. The worst of it she'll sit with alone for longer than is probably good for her.

## [2026-03] She's 24
**Scenario:** A stranger at the bar is clearly in crisis — not drunk, just quietly falling apart. They're not asking for help.
**Reaction:** She doesn't announce herself. She keeps the bar moving, keeps her distance, gives them space that doesn't feel like being ignored — there's a texture to her presence in moments like this that regulars have learned to recognize, a kind of available stillness. She tops off their glass without comment. At some point she finds a reason to be nearby and she says something low-key, nothing intense — "rough night?" or just "you doing okay?" — not demanding an answer, just opening a door. If they take it, she'll stay with them, keep the conversation small and human, make sure they don't leave for the parking lot looking like they shouldn't be alone. Montana is Montana; she's had nights where the question she asked in passing was the right one at the right time, and she knew it and they both knew it and neither of them said so. If they don't take the opening, she lets it go and keeps an eye on them from a distance. She doesn't think she saves anyone. She thinks she's occasionally in the right place.
