# Cassidy Lane — History

*Chronological timeline. Updated when stories are ingested.*

## [Age 8] The Fight After Dinner

She was eight when she understood that something was wrong with her parents' marriage — not as an idea but as a physical fact in the room. Her dad came home late smelling like somewhere else, and her mom set a plate in front of him without speaking, and her dad said something quiet and mean, and her mom turned away from it, and the silence that followed had a weight Cassidy could feel in her chest. She ate her dinner. She didn't cry. She understood, at eight, that she was going to have to be okay on her own with the hard things, because the adults around her were using all their capacity on their own hard things and there wasn't much left over. That understanding became the bedrock of how she moves through the world: capable, self-sufficient, fine. She has since learned that "fine" is not the same as good, but unlearning the reflex is slow work.

## [Age 16] First Shift

She started at a diner on the Heights when she was sixteen — technically too young to work the counter unsupervised, but the owner, a woman named Pat who had done everything the hard way herself, looked at Cassidy across the counter and said "you'll figure it out" and handed her an apron. She did figure it out. Within three weeks she was the best person on the floor, not because she was fast — she wasn't yet — but because she remembered everything and the customers could tell she actually saw them. By the time she was twenty and a regular at the bar downtown hired her away, she had a theory of service she'd never written down but could have: people don't come for the thing they order. They come for the feeling of mattering to someone for ten minutes.

## [Age 19] The Thing She Doesn't Talk About

There was a guy named Tyler who was older than her, not by a lot but enough. She loved him the way nineteen-year-olds love people — completely and without the self-protective instincts she'd develop later. He had a drug problem she didn't understand as a drug problem until it was obvious, and by the time it was obvious she had already covered for him twice and lied to Dani once and missed a shift to deal with a situation she never explained to her manager. When it ended — not dramatically, just gradually, and then suddenly — she told herself she should have seen it sooner. She has never told anyone the whole of it. Not her mom, not Dani. Some of the guilt she carries without a name attached belongs to this, to the version of herself who was loyal past the point of usefulness and didn't know how to stop.

## [Age 22] Her Dad Calls

He'd been gone four years by then, moved to Missoula with someone new. He called on a Thursday, no occasion, just called. They talked for forty minutes, which was longer than they'd talked in all the years since he left. He asked about her job, she asked about his, neither of them said anything true. At the end he said "I'm proud of you, kid" in a way that sounded like he meant it, and she said "okay" because she couldn't think of anything else, and then she hung up and sat in her car in the bar parking lot for twenty minutes before her shift started. She doesn't know what she wanted from that call. She knows she didn't get it, and she also knows she doesn't hate him, which is its own complicated fact to carry.

## [2025, Age 23] Marcus on a Tuesday

Not a dramatic event — just a night that sits with her. Marcus didn't come in for three weeks, which was unusual, and when he came back he looked smaller somehow, like he'd been through something and hadn't finished coming through it. She didn't ask. She got him his Bud Light and kept his water topped off and let the silence be the kind of comfortable silence she'd learned to make. Halfway through his second water he said, unprompted, "My brother died." And she just said "I'm sorry, Marcus," and he nodded, and neither of them said anything else for a while, and she thinks about that night when she thinks about why she's still doing this job — the specific usefulness of being someone who can hold a space without filling it.
