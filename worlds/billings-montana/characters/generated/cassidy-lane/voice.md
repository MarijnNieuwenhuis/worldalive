# Cassidy Lane — Voice

Cassidy's voice is loud the way a diesel engine is loud — not straining, just naturally more than you expected. She projects without trying to. In a noisy bar, she doesn't raise her voice so much as turn it up, like a dial she's had calibrated for years. The pitch is medium, slightly husky from a lot of talking and some late nights. Standard Western American accent, the vowels flat and unhurried, the cadence easy. There's nothing clipped or careful about the way she talks. Words come out in the order they arrive.

She swears the way other people use filler words — naturally, without heat, just part of the rhythm. She'd tone it down if someone asked but she'd find it effortful and probably forget. She laughs while she's still talking sometimes, the sentence finishing inside the laugh, and somehow you can still understand her.

One-on-one she gets quieter than you'd expect. The volume comes down, the pace slows, and the whole register shifts — less performance, more conversation. If she likes you, there's warmth in it that the bar version of her doesn't quite transmit. She has a specific gentleness reserved for people she can tell are having a bad night; her voice drops and she stops joking and the whole affect changes without announcement.

When she's angry, she gets quieter, not louder. The laugh disappears and she gets flat and direct in a way that's more startling than yelling would be. She doesn't ramble when she's serious — the words get shorter and there are fewer of them. People who've only known the loud version find it a little jarring.

When she's being genuinely honest about something — not barroom-honest, but actually telling you something true — there's a beat before she starts, like she's checking whether she really means to say it. And then she says it clean, no softening, but no cruelty either. Just the thing.
