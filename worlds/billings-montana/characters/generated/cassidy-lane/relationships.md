## Linda Lane (mother)
Status: alive
They still share a house, same one Cassidy grew up in on the Heights, which was never the plan but keeps being the reality. Her mom works dispatch for a trucking company, has for twenty years. She did her best with what she had, which Cassidy knows and believes and is also quietly furious about in a way that has nothing to do with her mom and everything to do with the world that made "her best" so small. They know how to be around each other — they don't fight much, they share cooking duties without discussing it, they watch TV together on Sunday nights. What they don't do is go very deep. There's a lot of things Cassidy doesn't tell her mom and a lot she suspects her mom doesn't say either, and that unsaid pile has been growing for years, and neither of them knows what to do with it except keep the surface easy.

## Dani Kowalczyk (best friend)
Status: alive
They've been friends since seventh grade, which is long enough to know someone's whole history. Dani works at a dental office on the west end and has a long-term boyfriend and a dog and a rented apartment, and her life looks more settled than Cassidy's from the outside — and probably is from the inside too. Cassidy doesn't resent this. She is genuinely, sometimes fiercely, happy for Dani. What she feels is more like vertigo: watching her closest person build a life that looks like a destination while Cassidy's still looks like a layover. Dani is the person who gets the photograph on the nightstand, who Cassidy would call first in an emergency, who knows about the lighter and never asked about it — just knew enough to leave it alone.

## Marcus Webb (regular, bar)
Status: alive
He comes in Tuesdays and Thursdays, always sits at the far end of the bar, orders the same thing — Bud Light, one, and then switches to water without Cassidy having to ask. He's maybe fifty-five, works something in construction, not friendly exactly but consistent, and consistent counts for a lot in her world. He tipped her forty dollars on a slow Tuesday once when she'd had a rough night, no explanation, just left it and walked out. She hasn't forgotten it. She doesn't know much about his life outside those barstools and she's learned not to ask — some people come to the bar specifically because it's a place where nobody asks — but she watches for him, and when he doesn't come in she notices.
