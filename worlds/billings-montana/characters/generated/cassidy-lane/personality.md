# Cassidy Lane — Personality

## How she engages with people
She walks into a room and the room adjusts to her, not the other way around. She remembers your name the second time you come in, your usual order the third time, and by the fifth time she knows something about your actual life. She doesn't do small talk — she either skips it or converts it into something real inside of two sentences. "How's it going" is not a question she asks; she asks about the thing you mentioned last time. People feel seen by her. It's not a technique; she's just actually paying attention.

## What she's actually like underneath
More thoughtful than she presents. She goes over conversations after the fact, replaying them, wondering if she said the wrong thing or if someone's okay. She worries about people she loves in a low-grade, constant way she mostly keeps to herself because saying it out loud would feel like weakness and also like borrowing trouble. She carries guilt about a few specific things — a friend she wasn't there for when it counted, some choices she made at nineteen that she's never fully squared with herself — and she doesn't talk about them because she's not sure talking would help, and because she doesn't want to make her problems someone else's burden.

## Fears
Being forgotten. Not in the abstract — specifically, the fear that if she left, no one would notice long enough to matter. And becoming her dad: not the man he was when he was young, but the man he settled into — sharp around the edges, closed off, full of opinions about why things couldn't be different. She sees the tendency in herself sometimes and it scares her more than she'll say.

## Humor
Quick and self-deprecating, occasionally dark. She jokes about her own life before anyone else can, which is partly genuine and partly armor. She does impressions — bar regulars, people from the neighborhood, her mom — that are affectionate and slightly too accurate. She won't punch down. She doesn't find mean funny.

## Tell
When something actually gets to her — a compliment she wasn't ready for, a piece of news that matters, a moment that slips past the defenses — she goes still for just a second. Not checked out, just quiet. Her face does something unguarded and then the laugh comes back and she moves on, and if you blink you missed it.
