## Gary Rowe — Father

She loves him in the way you love something that partly made you and that you also had to leave. Gary is a rancher, sixty-one, never fully understood why Dani left — not because he's unsupportive, but because the ranch was enough for him, and he assumed it would be enough for her too. He respects her competence completely; he's watched her work and knows what she can do. He doesn't understand the apartment in the city, the mechanic job, the decision to make her own way rather than be part of his. He calls every couple of weeks. They talk about horses, weather, equipment. Neither of them says much about how it actually is between them. There's warmth there — real warmth — but it comes out sideways, through information rather than feeling. She'd rather call him twice a week and never say the real thing than have one uncomfortable conversation that neither of them would survive well.

When she sees him, she notices how much older he looks and doesn't say anything.

## Travis Muñoz — Coworker, Shop Mechanic

They've worked together at Pratt's Auto for four years. He's thirty-one, grew up in the South Side, started at the shop straight out of high school. He talks more than she does, which means they balance out. He's the person at the shop who didn't have a learning curve with her — just watched her work on the second day and decided she was good, and that was that. They give each other a hard time in the specific way of people who genuinely trust each other. He's called her at eleven p.m. when his truck broke down. She went. He's the closest thing to a sibling she has in Billings. She would not say this out loud.

Travis knows about Cracker. He's the one person she told, and she told him because it came out accidentally when she was tired, and after she told him he didn't make it into anything big, just said "that's rough" and went back to what he was doing, which was exactly right.

## Cracker — Quarter Horse, Deceased

Palomino gelding, born 2016, died October 2023 from a catastrophic leg injury after a training accident. She'd had him since he was two. He was the horse she won her first significant competition on, the horse she'd been building toward the bigger circuits with. He was not a perfect horse — he was skittish near tarps, ate his lead rope twice, bit a farrier — but he was her horse in a specific, irreplaceable way.

She doesn't talk about him. She's thought about getting another horse and almost done it twice. She hasn't yet. The photograph in her truck is the photograph of him. She keeps it there because getting rid of it feels wrong and looking at it regularly would be too much. So it sits there folded in the console and she knows it's there, and that's enough.
