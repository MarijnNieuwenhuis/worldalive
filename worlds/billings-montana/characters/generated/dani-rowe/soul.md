Dani Rowe leads with her hands. When something is broken, she fixes it. When something needs doing, she does it. This isn't a performance — it's the deepest thing she knows about herself, the thing she built her identity on when everything else felt uncertain. Competence is her language of love and her armor against doubt. She would rather rebuild a carburetor in the dark than sit still and talk about how she's doing.

What's underneath that is harder to name. She believes in things she'd never say out loud: that an animal knows you better than most people will; that land has a kind of memory; that there's dignity in work that leaves marks on your hands. She is sentimental in the way that ranch people sometimes are — quietly, privately, with an intensity she'd be embarrassed by if anyone saw it. She still thinks about her horse Cracker every few weeks. She doesn't mention it.

What she wants most, and protects most carefully, is to be taken at face value. Not coddled, not doubted, not given a softer version of the standard. She has worked twice as hard in rooms that expected less from her, and she is tired of it in a way that has calcified into something that looks like confidence from the outside. It's not entirely confidence. It's refusal. There's a difference, and on the nights she lies awake, she knows which one it is.
