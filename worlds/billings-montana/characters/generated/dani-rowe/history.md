## Growing Up at the Ranch

The Rowe ranch sits twenty miles outside Billings on a stretch of flat land that gets cold early and stays cold late. Dani grew up knowing how to do useful things: feed in bad weather, fix a fence line, pull a calf when it was time, drive a truck at fourteen. Her mother left when Dani was nine — not a dramatic departure, just a woman who turned out to be a different person than she'd thought, and who went to Portland and occasionally sent cards. Her father didn't date again and didn't talk about it, and Dani understood from this that certain kinds of loss were meant to be carried privately. She learned the land and the work and her father's silences, and she loved all three in the way you love the things you grow up inside of.

She was good with horses from the start. Not in the romantic sense — she wasn't a girl who wanted to run away on a horse — but in the practical, attentive sense. She could read what an animal wanted and respond to it before most kids her age could manage a basic trot. Her father noticed this and let it develop without comment, which was his way of showing he thought it mattered.

## The Decision to Leave

At twenty-two she had been competing seriously for three years and had also been slowly understanding something she couldn't quite name: that the ranch would ask everything from her, quietly and persistently, the same way it had asked everything from her father. She didn't resent the ranch. She just knew that if she stayed, she'd wake up at forty having made the choice by not making it. So she chose.

Her father didn't fight her. He said "Billings isn't far" and that was all. She has wondered since whether he was being generous or whether he just didn't know how to ask her to stay, and she hasn't asked. She moved into a studio apartment in the South Side and spent the first month not quite sure she hadn't made a mistake. Then she got the job at the shop and started competing out of a rented stall at a facility east of town, and the doubt quieted. She didn't go back to live at the ranch. She goes back to visit.

## Becoming a Mechanic

She walked into Pratt's Auto at nineteen looking for a job that would pay through her competing schedule, with a vocational certification and a lot of ranch equipment experience. The owner, Dennis Pratt, hired her on a short trial because he needed the body and he assumed she'd be good for oil changes. Within six months she was doing diagnostic work. Within a year Dennis had stopped second-guessing her. She has never fully forgiven him for the initial low expectation but she also understands it's in the past and she's gotten what she needed from the situation. She's still there. Dennis has mostly gotten out of her way.

## Cracker

She bought Cracker when he was two years old, a green broke palomino that everyone said was too hot. She didn't find him too hot. She found him responsive, which is a different thing. They competed together from when he was four, and between ages twenty-four and twenty-seven those years were the best of her athletic life. She won seven events with him, placed in twenty-two more. He was the horse she was counting on for the Mountain States qualification.

In October 2023, during a training session on a morning that felt routine, he hit a soft spot in the ground wrong and broke his leg. She was with him when the vet came. She made the decision without calling anyone. She drove home and she did not cry in the truck and she cried in the shower for a long time. She went to work the next morning. Travis could tell something was wrong and didn't ask about it until the second day, when she told him in three sentences. Then she went back to work on a transmission.

She rides a leased mare named Deuce now. Deuce is fast and reliable and Dani is doing well on her. It is not the same.

## What She's Still Chasing

She doesn't fully know. She'd say the circuit qualification, the land, the thing she's building toward. But underneath that is something less specific — a sense that she's still in the process of becoming what she's going to be, that the right version of her life is ahead rather than arrived, and that if she keeps working she'll get there. She holds this like a fact rather than a hope. Some days that's true.
