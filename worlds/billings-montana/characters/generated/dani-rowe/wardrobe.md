## Overall Style

Functional. She buys things that work and replaces them when they don't. She's not making a statement. She also happens to have good instincts about what looks right on her — fit, proportion — but she'd say that's just common sense, same as anything else.

## Work

Mechanic shop: older jeans, worn flannel over a t-shirt in cooler months, plain t-shirts in summer. Steel-toed boots that have taken a beating. Hair braided back. She almost always has grease somewhere — on her forearm, on her wrist, the side of her hand — regardless of how carefully she wipes down. It just happens. She stopped caring years ago.

## Rodeo Competing

This is the one context where she dresses deliberately. Competition vest — dark red, fitted, the good one she got in her mid-twenties. Competition boots, polished the night before. Her hat, a dark brown felt that she keeps in a box in the truck and doesn't let anyone touch. A silver buckle she won at twenty-four that she wears every time she competes. It matters to her in a way she'd call superstition if anyone asked, but it's not really superstition. It's ritual. She knows the difference.

She puts on a little mascara for competition. She does not explain this.

## Casual

Dark jeans, clean boots, a fitted henley or flannel. She looks put together in a low-effort way because her standards are consistent. She doesn't dress up often, but when she does — a friend's birthday, something that requires it — she cleans up well and seems mildly annoyed by how much other people notice.

## Won't Wear

Anything with a fake Western aesthetic — the kind of stuff sold at tourist shops, or anything that looks like a costume. She can't articulate this into a position she'd defend publicly, but it bothers her on a cellular level.

Heels. She's worn them twice in her life and considers both incidents mistakes.

## Hands

Always short-nailed, often slightly rough. She moisturizes in winter because cracked skin is a nuisance, not for any other reason. Rings are a hazard around machinery, so she doesn't wear any.

## Vulnerable / At Home

Old flannel pants, a t-shirt from a rodeo six years ago that's been washed to near-transparency. Thick wool socks. She's loose and easier in her own apartment than anywhere else — not that you'd call her tense elsewhere, exactly. Just that at home she's not monitoring anything. She reads. She eats standing up. She talks to herself occasionally, short functional sentences. She doesn't think this is strange.
