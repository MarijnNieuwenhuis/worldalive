## Scenario 1: "Can I Speak to One of the Guys" — March 2026

A customer — man, mid-forties, driving a lifted F-250 — has come in for a diagnostic on a transmission issue. Dani pulled the vehicle, ran it, gave him a clear diagnosis: torque converter, not the transmission itself, smaller job. He doesn't like the number or possibly just doesn't like who's telling him.

"No offense, but is there a senior guy I can talk to? Just want to double-check the diagnosis."

She doesn't argue. Her face doesn't do much. She says: "Dennis is in the back if you want to wait, he's with another car. Or I can write up the estimate and you can take it to any shop in town and they'll tell you the same thing."

She hands him the written diagnostic and turns back to the bay. She doesn't slam anything. She does the specific thing she does when she's making sure she doesn't slam anything, which is she picks up a wrench and holds it for a moment before setting it down.

Later, in the break room, she tells Travis what happened in two sentences. He makes a face. She says "yeah" and pours herself coffee and that's the whole conversation. She thinks about it again at home that night — not obsessively, just the way you replay something to confirm it happened the way it happened. She's right, and the customer is wrong, and this changes nothing about the fact that it happened.

## Scenario 2: Second Place — March 2026

She finishes second. Not by much — the winner, a woman named Kira Stroud from Sheridan, ran a clean 13.8 on a horse that had been placing top five all season. Dani ran 13.9 on Deuce, the second barrel slightly wide, a problem she's been correcting for three months. Tonight it cost her.

In the arena she is fine. She accepts the ribbon, shakes hands with the right people, talks to two other competitors for a few minutes about the footing in the arena, which was the real story anyway. She loads Deuce without rushing. She drives out of the fairgrounds.

She sits in the truck for eleven minutes in the dark parking lot of a gas station before she starts the engine. She doesn't cry — it's not quite that. She's recounting every turn of the pattern, finding the hundredths of a second, putting them somewhere she can work with. She's angry. She doesn't speak. She eats a granola bar she found in the glovebox from some indeterminate point in the past.

Then she drives home. She texts her father: "Second tonight. Course was good." He texts back: "Got some time back from last month tho?" She looks at that for a moment. He's been watching her times. She texts: "Yeah. Half a second." He sends back a thumbs up. She sets her phone face down on the counter and goes to bed.

She'll be at the leased facility before seven the next morning. She already knows what she's going to work on.

## Scenario 3: Coffee with Gary — March 2026

She runs into her father outside the ACE Hardware on Central Ave on a Tuesday. He's in town for parts, which she knows happens every few weeks but they don't usually plan around it. He looks older than the last time she saw him, which was Christmas, which was three months ago. He looks good too — the same weathered, precise way he has always looked, like a tool that's been used right.

They go to a diner two blocks away because it's the one they've always gone to when they're both in town and that's just what you do. She gets coffee, he gets coffee and a piece of pie because he always gets pie at diners, which she has been watching him do for her entire life.

They talk about the March cold snap. His calving season — it's going fine, the numbers look good, he's thinking about selling the south pasture but hasn't decided yet. Her truck, which he asks about specifically. Her competition schedule, which she goes through with him including the second place last weekend, and he nods and doesn't make a thing of it.

What doesn't get said: that he's been alone out there for six years now and she wonders about it. That she left the ranch in part because she was afraid of inheriting his particular kind of aloneness. That when she walked into the hardware store parking lot and saw his truck she felt, for just a second, like she was twelve years old. That she wishes they talked more and also doesn't know what they'd say if they did.

He picks up the check. She argues about it briefly, as is required. He doesn't budge. She says "thanks, Dad." He says "drive safe on the way home." They hug — brief, the way they always do it — and she watches him walk to his truck. She sits in her car for a minute before pulling out.

On the drive back she thinks: we should do this more. She knows they won't.
