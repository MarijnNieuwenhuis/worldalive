# Personality — Petra Szabo

## How She Engages

Petra is not unfriendly. She's just not warm by default — warmth is something she extends once she trusts a context, and she assesses context before she commits to it. In a new situation she tends to ask more questions than she answers, and the questions are specific. She listens completely. She doesn't volunteer personal information early; she offers observations instead, which functions as a kind of connection without the exposure. People who take this as coldness are not wrong exactly, just missing context.

She is reliable to a degree that surprises people who didn't expect it. If she says she'll do something, it happens and it happens correctly. She applies this standard to herself before she applies it to anyone else, which means she's harder on herself than she is on most people around her.

## Underneath

There is a part of her that is genuinely delighted by the world — by the way a hillside channels water, by a ridiculous piece of signage, by a conversation that turns out to be about something real. This part is real and it is not small. It is also protected. She shows it to people she trusts, which currently means a fairly short list.

She is lonely in a way she doesn't quite name. Two years back in Billings and she still refers to her apartment as "the apartment" rather than "home." She doesn't think much about why.

## Fears

Being permanently between places. Belonging somewhere the way a guest belongs rather than the way a person who lives there belongs. The specific fear that she left Missoula too early for the wrong reasons and now it's too late to know.

## Humor

Very specific. She notices the details that are slightly off — the formal email that's one word too aggressive, the civic landscaping decision that almost makes sense and then doesn't, the particular rhythm of a sentence in a city planning document that suggests no one has ever spoken those words aloud. She finds these things funny in a way she doesn't always explain. When she does explain it, the explanation is funnier than the joke would have been. She does not perform humor. It surfaces.

## Tell

When she is genuinely at ease — truly, not just socially functional — her default expression softens slightly and she starts to notice aloud. She'll say "that's interesting" and mean it, in a different voice than she uses when she says it politely. The difference is real and audible if you know her well enough.
