# Scenarios — Petra Szabo
*March 2026, age 32*

---

## Scenario 1: The Client Meeting

Petra is presenting a residential redesign — a backyard slope with drainage issues and an existing retaining wall the client wants gone. She's spent two weeks on this. The drawings are careful; she's walked through the grading logic in her head enough times that she can answer any question without notes.

Halfway into the presentation, the client — a man in his mid-fifties, general contractor, pleasant enough — stops looking at her and starts directing his questions to Kevin, her colleague, who is sitting to her left and who has been to the site exactly once. Kevin is good about it; he says "Petra would know" and gestures back toward her. The client nods, glances at her, then frames his next question toward Kevin again.

She finishes the presentation. She answers every question directly, looks at the client when she speaks, and does not perform impatience. Kevin catches her eye once and she gives him nothing because there's nothing available she'd want to give in this room right now. Afterward, in the parking lot, Kevin says "I'm sorry about that." She says "It happens." She doesn't say: *it always happens, and every time I decide whether to make it a thing and every time I decide it's not worth it and every time I wonder if that decision is cowardice or just efficiency.* She says "It happens" and means all of that in the specific tone she uses for those words.

## Scenario 2: The Party

Marcus invited her. She said yes because she's trying to do that more. The party is in a renovated house near downtown — people she doesn't know, some she recognizes, someone has a beer flight set up on the kitchen counter, the music is at the right volume for conversation. She's been there twenty minutes and is doing fine, which for her means she's found a corner that gives her a sightline to the whole room and she's been talking to two people about a road project on the Heights that she actually has opinions about.

Then someone new joins the group — friendly, slightly drunk, the particular sociability of someone who is having a genuinely good time. He asks her name. She says it. He says "Szabo — that's not from around here, is it." Not hostile. Just curious. The kind of question that is innocent and asked fifty times and still, somehow, lands the same way every time.

She gives him the answer she gives: her parents are from Hungary, she grew up here actually, her dad has been in Billings since '96. He says "Oh wow, cool, so you're like — fully American." She says "Yes" in her even voice. He moves on cheerfully and she stays in the conversation and contributes to it normally and does not think about it again tonight. Later, driving home, she thinks about it. The question itself is fine. The question is always fine. The accumulated math of the question is what she doesn't have a word for.

## Scenario 3: The Call

Tuesday evening. She's reviewing CAD files at her kitchen table when her mother calls. She switches to Hungarian without noticing. They talk about ordinary things for a while — her mother's week, a neighbor's new dog, something that happened at the dental office. Then her mother says, in a slight different register, that her father has been having some tiredness. Some shortness of breath. He's seen the doctor and the doctor isn't concerned, says it's probably this, probably that, they're going to monitor it.

"Probably," her mother says.

Petra asks specific questions. When was the appointment. What were the results. What is the follow-up plan and when. Her mother answers each one carefully and Petra can hear, in the way her mother is being careful, that she wanted to tell someone this, and the someone she wanted to tell was Petra. They end the call with her mother saying he's fine, really, don't worry. Petra says she's not worried. Both of these things are mostly true.

After she hangs up she sits at the table for a while without looking at the files. Her father is sixty-one years old and has never, in her memory, complained about anything physical. She thinks about the ratio of things she knows about him versus the full shape of who he is. She thinks about how much of her own quietness is his. She doesn't call back. She opens the CAD file. She'll call on Sunday like always, and the call will be about practical things, and she'll listen to the way he breathes without seeming to listen to it. She is very good at this kind of attention. She has been practicing it for years.
