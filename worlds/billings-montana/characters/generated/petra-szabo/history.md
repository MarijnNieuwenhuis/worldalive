# History — Petra Szabo

## The Name

In elementary school she was Peh-TRA to everyone, always. She corrected it once, in third grade, said the actual thing — PEH-tra — and the teacher said "okay, Peh-TRA" and moved on. She stopped correcting. Szabo was worse. Szabo became SAY-bo, SAW-bo, occasionally just "your last name" from teachers who glanced at it and decided not to commit. She learned to answer to all of it. By the time she was in high school she'd developed something like expertise in the moment when someone was about to say her name incorrectly — a particular fractional delay, a small intake of breath before the attempt. She got good at preparing her face. This is not a wound she carries conspicuously. It is a thing that made her careful about the distance between what things are called and what they are.

## The Choice to Go to Missoula

She could have stayed closer — Montana State was an option, people in Billings went there. But Missoula had the architecture and landscape architecture programs worth wanting, and more than that, it felt like a door she could walk through alone. She was eighteen and wanted to know if she could be someone different in a place that didn't already have a version of her filed away. She was good at school in a way that didn't translate socially, and she thought distance would fix that. It mostly did. She found out she could be different in a place that didn't know her, and what she became was quieter, more precise, more herself. This surprised her. She'd expected to relax.

## What She Was Building There

A life, she thought. A set of people who knew her, a city she understood, the beginning of a career that felt like it had a direction. She worked at a good firm. She had Dani and three or four other people who mattered. She was learning the specific language of Missoula — its neighborhoods, its particular self-mythology, the way the mountains sit around the city. She felt, for a few years, like she was becoming a local. Not effortlessly, but actually.

## Why She Came Back

Her mother called in the spring of 2023 and said her father had been diagnosed with something that turned out to be manageable but at the time wasn't framed that way. She started driving back on weekends. The firm in Billings called in August — a small practice, good work, the kind of project-driven office that does things worth doing. She framed it to everyone including herself as a career opportunity. It was also that her mother had asked, without asking, if she might come. She has not examined the ratio.

## What She Hasn't Built Here Yet

She's been back two years and she still has one real friend and a job she's good at and an apartment she rents and no particular relationship to the city she grew up in. She drives past her high school sometimes and feels exactly nothing, which is its own data. She went to a party in the new brewery district last fall and stood near people her age talking about Billings with proprietorial warmth — *this city is finally becoming something* — and she thought: I grew up here. But she didn't say it. She didn't know if it counted the way they meant it, or whether she wanted it to.
