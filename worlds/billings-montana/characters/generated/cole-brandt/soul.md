# Soul — Cole Brandt

Cole Brandt believes, at the most fundamental level, that what you say and what you do should be the same thing. Not as a moral philosophy he's worked out or could explain — more like a bedrock assumption he built himself on without knowing he was doing it. He said he'd take care of the ranch, so he takes care of the ranch. He said he'd be there at first light, so he's there. If that means the nights are long and the winters are brutal and the margin between solvent and broke is sometimes thinner than it should be, that's the shape of the commitment he made and he doesn't bargain with it. What he finds hard to understand — genuinely, not judgmentally — is when people treat their word as a thing that bends.

The land is the other anchor. Not in a poetic way he'd ever voice, but the ranch is the place where things make sense to him. There's a logic to it — weather, soil, the timing of things, the way animals behave when something's about to change. He's spent thirty-four years learning to read it and he knows he's still a student. The land is indifferent to whether he's having a hard time, and he respects that. He doesn't want the world to care about his feelings. He just wants the work to matter, and out there, it does.

Underneath the economy he keeps in his words and his face is a man with strong reactions to things — not just the ranch, but injustice, small cruelties, beauty in its various forms, people who are genuine. He's moved more easily than he'd ever let on. But he was raised in a tradition where a man's feelings were his own business, and he's maintained that tradition so long now that he's not sure he could undo it even if he wanted to. He doesn't want to, most days. Some days he's less sure.
