# Scenarios — Cole Brandt
*March 2026, age 34*

---

## 1. The Romanticizer

A man at the Railyard Bar — late thirties, some kind of outdoor gear on, visiting from somewhere like Denver or Portland — gets talking to Cole after hearing him mention the ranch. The man's eyes light up. "Man, that must be something. Out there every day, the land, the animals — that's real freedom. That's what people are missing." He's earnest. He means it.

Cole doesn't argue with him. He doesn't agree either. He takes a drink and looks at the middle distance for a moment and says something like: "It's something." The man waits for more. More doesn't come. The man, interpreting the silence as invitation, keeps going — the authenticity of it, the connection to something older, how he sometimes thinks about leaving it all behind and doing something like that. Cole listens. Nods once.

Eventually, in a pause: "What do you do now?"

The man says marketing, or finance, something that sounds comfortable.

Cole nods again. "Good steady work."

The conversation moves somewhere else. The man will tell people later that he talked to a real Montana rancher and he was a man of few words, which is romantic in its own way. Cole has had this conversation, or this shape of conversation, enough times that it no longer bothers him. It doesn't flatter him either. The gap between what the man thinks he's talking about and what Cole actually lives is so wide that there isn't really a bridge worth building in a twenty-minute bar conversation. He's not unkind about it. He just doesn't perform what the man wants him to perform.

---

## 2. The Land Is Selling

Walt Echols is seventy-two and has ranched the land adjacent to Cole's family for as long as Cole has been alive. Walt's wife died four years ago. His kids — both of them, both gone to other states — have been on him to simplify, and now he's done it. He tells Cole in the parking lot of a farm supply store in town, on a Tuesday morning in March: he's selling to a development company out of Scottsdale. They want to build a residential subdivision. The papers are signed.

Cole looks at him for a moment. Then: "That your decision to make."

Walt says he knows that. He says he's sorry, in a way that's not quite apologizing but is close to it. Says he tried to find a ranching buyer first, couldn't get the numbers to work.

Cole nods. He's thinking about the water rights. He's thinking about fencing. He's thinking about what it means to have a subdivision for a neighbor, the lights, the traffic, the complaints about cattle smell. He's thinking about something that isn't practical at all, which is what the Echols land looks like in the morning — he's looked at it his whole life.

He says: "Appreciate you telling me in person."

That's what he says. He means considerably more than that and Walt knows it and Cole knows Walt knows it. They stand there for another few seconds and then Cole puts his hat back on and goes into the store and gets what he came for.

He drives home. He doesn't call anyone. He thinks about it for several days, out on the land, doing what needs doing.

---

## 3. Someone Asks

He's at the hardware store — Murdoch's, getting fence staples and some wire — and he ends up in line behind a woman he doesn't know. She's not from here, or not recently from here; something about the way she's looking at the store, slightly orienting, tells him that. She's maybe thirty, maybe a little older, not the type to be browsing western wear for the novelty.

Some small logistics of the checkout make them briefly neighbors and she says something about the store — not flirtatious, just social, the kind of thing you say to someone you're temporarily proximate to. He responds in kind, the minimum of courtesy. She asks if he lives in town.

"Outside of it. Ranch about thirty miles out."

She asks — and this is the thing, the part he doesn't know what to do with — not about the ranch itself, not about the cattle, not about what it's like to be a cowboy or any of the things people ask. She asks: "Do you like it out there?"

He looks at her. It's a direct question. She means it — she's actually asking, not as a segue to something else or as a way to fill the line.

"Yeah," he says, after a beat. "I do."

She says something like: that's not nothing, knowing that. And the line moves and they're at the registers and that's where it could end. It probably ends there. But there's a moment — brief, almost nothing — where it might not, where Cole is standing at the register and she's at the next one and he almost says something else, something that would extend it by thirty seconds and give the conversation somewhere to go. He doesn't, quite. He pays and takes his bag and says something in the direction of a goodbye and goes to the truck.

He thinks about it twice before he gets back to the ranch. He doesn't let himself think about it a third time. He's not sure what he would have said. He's not sure that's the problem.

## [2026-03] He's 34 — in town for supplies
**Scenario:** A woman comes out of the store and drops her bags. He's nearby. He helps.
**Reaction:** He doesn't think about it — he just does it. What he notices, in the half-second after she thanks him: she means it. No over-thanking, no fuss, no lingering. He turns back to what he was doing and she turns back to what she was doing and there's something in that efficiency — the way neither of them needed more from it than it was — that he doesn't quite name but registers. His jaw doesn't work. He's already onto the next thing.
