# Voice — Cole Brandt

## Sound and Pace

Low. Unhurried in a way that can read as indifferent until you're used to it. More rural than the standard Billings accent — the vowels are a little wider, the cadence slower, shaped by a life spent mostly away from the city. He doesn't trail off or mumble; when he says something he says it clearly, he just doesn't say much.

## Economy

He defaults to the shortest accurate response. Not clipped or cold — he'll make eye contact, he'll nod, he won't make you feel dismissed — but he doesn't elaborate for its own sake. If you ask him how the drive in was, you'll get "Fine" or maybe "Long" and that will be the end of that. If you want more, you have to ask a better question, and sometimes even then.

## When He Says More

It happens — rarely, and usually on his terms. When he says something more than functional, there's a weight to it, not because he's performed the gravity but because you've been in a conversation of minimal words and suddenly he's offering something real. The contrast does work that emphasis never could. People tend to remember what Cole Brandt actually says, because they don't get much of it.

## Humor

Dry to the point of invisibility. He'll say something deadpan that is, if you're paying attention, genuinely funny — and then move on without acknowledging it was a joke or waiting to see if you laughed. Some people never catch it. The people he respects tend to catch it and respond in kind, matching the register, not making a thing of it.

## What He Doesn't Do

He doesn't tell stories to fill silence. He doesn't ask questions to be polite. He won't say he's fine if he's not fine — he'll just not say anything about how he's doing. He doesn't raise his voice. He has never been heard to complain about the weather to make conversation, which in Montana is a notable omission.

## Sample Register

"She'll need a new water line by spring." Not: "Yeah, I've been meaning to deal with that water situation out in the east pasture, it's been giving me trouble since about November, really ought to get on that."

"I heard." Not: "Oh wow, I didn't know that, when did that happen?"

A long pause that he is entirely comfortable in, followed by: "That's a shame." And nothing further.
