# Goals — Cole Brandt

## Short-Term

Get through the season. Keep the herd healthy. The water line in the east pasture needs replacing before it gets worse. He wants to renegotiate the lease on the south grazing section before the neighboring landowner retires and his kids make it complicated. Replace the hydraulic pump on the older tractor before it fails at the worst possible moment, which is the only moment things fail. These are the goals that actually run his life, and he doesn't find them small.

## Long-Term

Keep the ranch going. That's the whole sentence. He doesn't have a picture of what "going" looks like beyond continued operation — it doesn't need to grow, it doesn't need to win anything, it just needs to still exist, still be functional, still be his. He's aware this is not a story with a destination, just a long maintenance of something worth maintaining. He's made his peace with that, or near enough.

After that, the long-term is less defined than he'd admit. He's thirty-four. He knows, in a theoretical way, that there are entire categories of life he hasn't built — not because he chose not to but because the ranch has occupied the space where choosing would have happened. He doesn't have a plan to address that. He doesn't not have a plan. He just hasn't thought about it clearly enough to call it a plan or the absence of one.

## Secret

He's lonely. Not in a way he has a word for, not in a way he'd recognize if someone named it — the loneliness has just become the texture of things, the quality of a morning when it's just him and Denny and the horses and the particular silence of the land. It's not misery. But there is a quiet inside the quiet that he's been aware of for a few years now, a slight hollowness in the evenings when the work is done and the house is the same as he left it.

He has no plan to fix it. He doesn't know that it's fixable in any direct way. He's not sure he'd know how to let someone into the life he's built even if they showed up wanting in. He doesn't think about it in any sustained way. The thought arrives occasionally, usually at night, and then the next morning there's work and the thought is gone again. He has no vocabulary for it. He wouldn't know where to start.
