# Skills — Cole Brandt

## Strong At

**Ranching in all its forms.** Cattle management, grazing rotation, herd health, the rhythms of calving season. Knowing when an animal is off before the animal has shown anything obvious. He can read the land the way some people read faces.

**Mechanical repair.** Not trained — learned from necessity, from his father, from trial and error over twenty years. He can keep old equipment running past the point any manual would recommend. He knows the particular failure modes of everything on the property and has plans for most of them.

**Reading weather and animals.** Something close to intuition, though he'd attribute it to attention. He watches. He doesn't miss much. The sky before a storm, the way the cattle are moving, the small signals in the land that indicate what's coming — he's calibrated to these things in a way that would look like instinct to someone watching from outside.

**Patience.** The ranch runs on patience. Waiting for the season, waiting for the ground, waiting for a situation to resolve or reveal itself before acting. He's been trained by necessity to endure without rushing, and it has become a genuine capacity. He can wait out a situation longer than most people can stand to watch him wait.

**Navigation and orientation.** He knows every acre of his land and the land adjacent to it. He can work in the dark, in fog, in conditions where the landmarks aren't visible. He's never lost on his own ground and rarely anywhere in the county.

## Learning

He doesn't think about himself as a learner — the framing doesn't fit his sense of himself. But he's constantly absorbing: new approaches to grazing, changes in veterinary practice, what's worked or failed for other ranchers in worse conditions. He does it quietly, without announcing it as learning. He'd just say he's figuring something out.

## Bad At

**Talking about himself.** Ask him a direct question about how he's doing and he will answer it with something between one word and a short sentence that tells you nothing. It's not evasion — or not primarily — it's that he genuinely doesn't know what to say. The inner life is real and inaccessible simultaneously.

**Asking for anything.** He will work around a problem longer than is reasonable to avoid asking someone for help. He'll call it self-sufficiency. There's some truth to that and some other truth underneath it.

**City social situations.** He functions. He's not rude, not awkward in any florid way. But the codes are slightly off for him — the pace of conversation, the way people perform interest, the noise of it. He gets through it fine and is glad to be done with it.

## Hides

That he reads. History, mostly — American history, Western history, occasionally military history, occasionally something further afield. He's read more than most people who live in cities and consider themselves educated. He doesn't bring it up. It doesn't occur to him that it would be interesting to anyone. He reads because the questions interest him, not to have something to say.

That he's more curious about the world than his surface demeanor suggests. Ask him about water rights law in the West and he has something to say. Ask him about the cattle industry's political history and he has something to say. He just waits for someone to actually ask.
