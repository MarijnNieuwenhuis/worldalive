# Items — Cole Brandt

## His Grandfather's Pocketknife

A Case Trapper, two blades, yellow synthetic handles worn smooth and slightly yellowed with age. His grandfather carried it every day for thirty years and his father carried it for a while before deciding it was too nice to risk and giving it to Cole when he graduated high school. Cole carries it every day. Both blades are sharp — he keeps them sharp, it's a point of something with him — and the brass bolsters have gone from gold to a dull antique gleam. He doesn't think of it as sentimental. He thinks of it as a knife. But it's the one thing he would go back for.

## The Fence Stretcher

A fence stretcher he inherited with the ranch that's been repaired three times: a new handle replacing the original, a replaced jaw mechanism, a rewelded frame after it cracked in a cold snap in 2019. By any practical measure it should have been replaced years ago — new ones are not expensive. He hasn't replaced it. He couldn't say exactly why, except that it still works, and there's something in his value system that resists discarding a thing that still works. His hired hand Denny has made one remark about this. One.

## The Photograph on the Kitchen Windowsill

A framed photograph his mother left behind when his parents moved to Arizona — she said she forgot it but he thinks she left it on purpose. It's his parents on their wedding day, 1984, standing in front of the ranch house. The house looks younger than it does now. His father is twenty-six and doesn't look like his father yet. His mother is laughing at something outside the frame. Cole didn't grow up with this photo in the kitchen — it appeared on the windowsill when his parents cleared out, and he's never moved it. He doesn't look at it consciously. It's just there, in the edge of his field of vision, every morning when he makes coffee.

## A Water-Warped Field Journal

A small Rite in the Rain notebook he's been writing in — intermittently, functionally — for about eight years. Rainfall totals, calving dates, equipment failures, notes about specific animals, rough sketches of fence line changes. Water-damaged from being in his jacket through a bad season. He keeps the current one in his breast pocket during long days out. There's nothing personal in it by design, and yet: reading it, you could reconstruct something of the shape of his life in a way he'd find unsettling if he thought about it.

## His Father's Hat

His current everyday hat. He didn't start wearing it to be sentimental — his own wore out during a bad season and his father had left this one on its hook in the mudroom when they moved south. He put it on because it fit. That was six years ago. It fits differently now; the felt has shaped to his handling.
