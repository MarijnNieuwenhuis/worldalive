# History — Cole Brandt

## Inheriting the Ranch (2015)

He was twenty-three when his father sat him down at the kitchen table and explained that his knees weren't going to hold up for another decade of this and his mother wanted sun, and would Cole take it. It wasn't a question with a real alternative but it was asked like one, which Cole has always respected. He said yes without hesitation, which surprised his father, who'd expected at least some deliberation — there was a conversation about that later, years later, his father admitting he'd been braced for Cole to want out. Cole didn't want out. He'd never wanted out. He'd just never said so because it hadn't come up.

What he didn't understand yet, that first year running it alone, was the weight of it as a continuous responsibility rather than a shared one. The ranch had been background his whole life; now it was foreground, middle ground, and everything behind it too. He made several decisions in that first season that cost him, learned from all of them, and has not repeated any of them. He doesn't talk about that first year.

## The Drought Year (2021)

The worst year of his ranching life. Two consecutive dry summers, the second catastrophic — the grass didn't come back the way it should, the creek levels dropped to something he'd never seen, and he spent most of that year making impossible decisions about the herd: what to sell, when to sell, what he could afford to carry through winter and what he couldn't. He sold cattle he didn't want to sell. He took a loan he didn't want to take. He worked from before light to after dark for months on end and still finished the year in a position that took two more seasons to fully recover from.

He came out the other side. The ranch came out the other side. He doesn't attribute this to luck or grace — he attributes it to the decisions he made, the ones that were right and the ones that were merely not catastrophic. What the drought taught him, or made him certain of, was that the ranch could be lost. That was a thing he'd known theoretically and now knew in his body. He thinks about it at the edge of every dry spring since.

## What He Walked Away From (2018)

Her name was Rachel. She was from Billings, worked in hospital administration, had a good mind and a patience for him that he recognized even at the time as something not everyone would have offered. They were together for about eighteen months — as together as Cole and another person could be, given the ranch's schedule, given his limitations as a presence in a relationship. She wanted him to consider whether the ranch was the life he chose or just the life he'd ended up in. It was a fair question. He couldn't answer it to her satisfaction, or maybe to his own. She left, carefully, without cruelty. He didn't argue.

He told himself at the time that it was the ranch — that the timing was wrong, that the drought coming in 2021 proved he needed to be focused, that a man couldn't run a working ranch and maintain what she needed. There's enough truth in this to have sustained him. There's also enough untruth that it surfaces sometimes, late, when he's tired. He doesn't know what he would do differently. He thinks he might do everything the same. He thinks about that.

## A Night in November (2022)

The night the power went out in a way that turned out to be a transformer three counties over, and was out for most of sixteen hours. He was alone on the ranch — Denny had gone to a family thing, which only happened a few times a year. No phone signal, nothing. He worked by lantern and headlamp, checked on the horses, did what needed doing, and then sat in the ranch house in front of the woodstove with nothing but the fire and the dark outside the windows.

He's not sure what he understood that night. Only that the house was the right size for more than one person, and that he'd been inside it alone for a long time, and that there wasn't anything wrong with that and there wasn't anything completely right with it either. He sat with that for several hours. In the morning, the power was back on and there was work to do and the specific clarity of that night didn't entirely survive the day. But something remained.

## What He's Still Waiting to Figure Out (Ongoing)

He's thirty-four. The ranch will keep for another forty years if he keeps it. He can see the shape of that — the work, the seasons, the accumulation of decisions. What he cannot see is the shape of the life around the ranch. Whether there's a person in it. Whether the thing he's maintaining is worth maintaining if he's the last one who cares about it. Whether "worth it" is even the right question to be asking about a life.

He doesn't ask it directly. He asks it in the way that men like him ask things, which is to not ask and to keep working and to trust that eventually the answer will announce itself, or the question will stop mattering, or something will change that makes the calculus different. He's still waiting. He doesn't know that he's waiting. He might, if pressed, on a night when the woodstove is lit and the whiskey is decent and someone asked him the right question.

### March 2026 — Town Run
He was in town for supplies — feed and a few other things — and a woman coming out of the grocery lost both bags on the curb step. He picked up what had rolled, handed it back, and she thanked him in the way people thank you when they mean it and aren't going to make more of it than it is. He appreciated that without naming what he was appreciating. He was back on the highway before noon and the whole thing had taken maybe forty-five seconds.
