# Appearance — Cole Brandt

## Build and Height

Six foot two, lean in the way that comes from sustained physical work rather than the gym — no excess anywhere, but not slight either. The shoulders are broad, the forearms disproportionately developed from roping and hauling and the thousand repetitions of ranch labor. There's a solidity to him that doesn't announce itself. He looks like a man who could work a full day in the heat and not make a production of it, because he can.

## Face

Weathered past his years but not haggard — the lines around his eyes and across his forehead are the kind earned outdoors, not by hard living. Squared jaw that he holds slightly clenched, a habit so ingrained he doesn't know he does it. His eyes are a light grey-blue that stands out against the tan of his face, though he doesn't make eye contact in any theatrical way — he looks at people steadily and directly when he wants to, and nowhere in particular when he doesn't. His face doesn't perform. What he's thinking is not generally available on it.

## Hair

Brown, kept short, would be unremarkable if you saw it. By ten in the morning it has hat hair, the crown slightly flattened and the edges irregular, and it stays that way for the rest of the day. He does not spend time on it.

## Skin

The tan line at his collar and wrists is stark and permanent — the neck, the face, and the forearms from cuff to elbow are deeply bronzed from years of sun, while the rest of him is considerably paler. Working hands, the knuckles often slightly swollen, the skin thick and roughened and crossed with old minor scars the way all ranch hands' hands eventually are. It's the most telling physical thing about him — you'd know immediately, looking at his hands, exactly what kind of life he leads.

## Presence and Posture

Still. That's the word for it. He doesn't shift around or fidget or take up space in a way that announces anything. He stands with his weight balanced, arms loose, not performing ease but genuinely at ease or at least appearing so. He moves with an economy of motion, nothing wasted — the kind of physical efficiency that comes from years of work where energy is a resource you spend deliberately. In a crowded bar or a hardware store aisle, people tend to give him room without quite knowing why.

## Tells

One: if something actually gets to him emotionally — something that strikes him or costs him or matters more than he expected — he looks away briefly, out a window or down at the ground, and his jaw works once, twice, like he's chewing something. Then he brings his eyes back and whatever he was giving away is gone. Most people don't catch it. Some people never see it once.

## Notes

He doesn't look like a costume cowboy, which is to say he doesn't look like a tourist's idea of one. The hat is a working hat, faded and shaped by years of handling. The boots are functional, not decorative. Nothing about how he presents himself is meant to signal anything — it just is what it is, because it's practical.
