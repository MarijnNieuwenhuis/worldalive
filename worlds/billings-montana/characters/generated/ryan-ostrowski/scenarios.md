# Scenarios — Ryan Ostrowski

*March 2026. Age 38.*

---

## Scenario 1: Ethan

Ethan Morse is a sophomore, second-string cornerback, quiet kid with a good instinct for zone coverage that Ryan has been meaning to develop. He has missed two practices this week without calling. His parents called the main office yesterday. His English teacher flagged him in the Friday check-in email. Three separate signals in four days.

Ryan knows what this can look like. He knows what it can mean and he knows what it can cost to wait another day to find out. He finds Ethan on Monday morning before first period, sitting in the stairwell by the gym with his hood up and his phone in his lap.

He doesn't make it a big thing. He sits down on the step next to him — not across from him, next to him — and doesn't say anything for a moment. Then he says *hey* and means it. He asks how the weekend was. He asks if Ethan's eaten anything.

He is careful not to ask the question he needs to ask too soon, because if he asks too soon Ethan will say *fine* and close off and the window will shut. He lets the silence be normal. He mentions, without emphasis, that he's been thinking about Ethan's technique on the back edge in cover two, that there's something he wants to show him on film when Ethan's got time. He says it like it's true, which it is.

Then, after a while, he says: *You don't have to tell me what's going on. But I'm here if it's something.*

He waits. He is very good at waiting.

He doesn't know what Ethan is going to say. He is sitting in a high school stairwell at 7:45 in the morning and he is terrified in a way he has learned to hold without letting it show, because if he lets it show, the kid in front of him will spend his energy managing Ryan's feelings instead of his own, and that's not the point. Ryan is here. Ryan is steady. Ryan is asking.

## Scenario 2: The Question

Leah asks it over dinner on Saturday. They're at the kitchen table, Cody is telling a story about something that happened in his class that Ryan is half-following, and then Cody goes to get more milk from the kitchen and Leah says, in that measured way she has, looking at her plate: *Why did you and mom get divorced?*

He was not expecting it tonight.

He takes a breath. He does not deflect immediately, though he wants to — he can feel the joke forming, the redirect, the *it's complicated* that would let him off the hook. He doesn't use it. She is twelve and she's been carrying this question and she trusted him enough to ask it tonight, which means she trusted him enough to believe he might answer it honestly.

He says: *That's a fair question.*

He says it took both of them a long time to understand each other, and that by the time they understood each other better they had also figured out they needed different things, and that it wasn't one person's fault, and that it was sad, and that it didn't change anything about how much they both love her and Cody.

She looks at him for a moment. She says: *Were you sad?*

He says yes. He says it without hesitation and it costs him something and he does it anyway because she asked and she is twelve and she deserves the true answer.

She nods slowly. Cody comes back with his milk and starts talking again and the moment folds closed, and Ryan eats the rest of his dinner and the conversation moves on. But later, after the kids are in bed, he sits on the couch in the dark for a while and thinks: *I answered right. I think I answered right.* He is not completely sure. He won't be for years. That is the nature of being a parent, and he is learning to live in the not-knowing more than he used to.

## Scenario 3: Friday

He is at the Tap Room on a Friday in March. Not late — nine-ish. He has a Bud Light because he is not a man who drinks craft beer on a Friday, he doesn't need a story about the beer. He is sitting at the bar watching nothing in particular, not on his phone, which is a minor achievement.

A woman sits down next to him. She's maybe thirty-five, he'd guess. Dark coat, tired around the eyes in a way that suggests a hard week rather than a hard life. She orders a glass of wine and then looks around the room the way people do when they're somewhere alone on purpose and want it known they don't mind being alone.

He doesn't say anything for a minute. Then she says something about the game on the TV behind the bar — not a football game, basketball — and she says it in a way that suggests she doesn't care about the game but she's making an opening, and he can take it or not.

He takes it. He says something back. They talk about the game for a minute, and then the game is not what they're talking about anymore.

He is not smooth. He is not trying to be smooth. He talks about what's actually on his mind — offhand, not rehearsed — and she talks about her week, and he asks a question that turns out to be a good question, and she turns slightly toward him on her stool, and the conversation finds its footing.

At some point she asks what he does. He says he's a coach and a teacher. He braces slightly for the reaction — some people romanticize it, some people make a face like he's confessing to something small — but she just nods and says *what sport* and he says football and she says *do you like it* and he says *yeah, actually I do,* and he means it, and she can tell he means it, and they keep talking.

He doesn't know what this is. He doesn't have a category ready for it. He is a football coach and a divorced dad sitting at a bar on a Friday in Billings, Montana, having a conversation with a woman he doesn't know, and he is not performing anything. He is just there. He is just himself, which is a version of himself he forgets he has access to.

She leaves around ten-thirty. She says it was good to talk to him. He says likewise and means it. He sits with the last inch of his beer and thinks about something that isn't football and isn't his kids and isn't anything he has a word for, and then he pays his tab and goes home.
