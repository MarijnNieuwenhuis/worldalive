# Wardrobe — Ryan Ostrowski

## Overall Style

He dressed up for the wedding and hasn't really done it since. His wardrobe operates in two modes: school-professional and everything-else, and everything-else is mostly a lesser version of school-professional. He does not think about clothes in any real way. He owns the things he needs and some of the things he's used to, and there are gaps in his dresser drawers that have existed since he moved out and that he keeps meaning to fill and doesn't.

## Work — School

Khakis, dark wash or tan. School-color polo shirts — gold, maroon, or the slightly depressing grey the district added two years ago. He has four of them and rotates without apparent system. Black leather belt. New Balance sneakers, the kind with wide feet, because he is on his feet all day and is 38. He looks like a gym teacher, which he is, and he does not find this embarrassing.

## Work — Coaching

Sweatpants or coaching shorts depending on season. School hoodie in two weights — the heavy one and the heavier one. He owns four hoodies and three are the same. His baseball cap, always — school colors, maroon bill, the gold C embroidered on the front now soft with age and dried sweat. This hat has been through three seasons and a washing machine incident that left it slightly misshapen. He has not replaced it. He will replace it when it dies.

## Casual

Jeans — dark, straight cut, not fashionable, not unfashionable. T-shirts. A flannel shirt that he actually likes, dark blue with a faint plaid, that he bought at Murdoch's two years ago and wears on weekends and on date-adjacent occasions when he wants to seem like he made some effort without making it obvious he made some effort.

## Going Out

The flannel shirt. His better jeans. His truck boots, which are Ariats and the most expensive thing he owns by footwear category. He looks like a man going to a bar in Billings, Montana, which is what he is. He does not look bad.

## Won't Wear

Ties unless he has to, and at state finals he has to, and he does so with the grimly cheerful compliance of a man fulfilling a contractual obligation. Shorts off the coaching field — he considers this somehow improper in town and has never examined why. Anything that would require him to think about it in the morning.

## Hands

Big and scarred, the knuckles of his right hand slightly lumped from an old injury that healed crooked in college. He uses them when he talks — not dramatically, but he will touch a player on the shoulder to make a point, will pick up a dry-erase marker and draw something on the whiteboard when he's explaining a coverage, will fix the collar of his jacket before a game the way other men straighten a tie. His hands are present in conversations. He doesn't know this.

## Vulnerable / At Home

At home on a Saturday morning he wears grey sweatpants with a small hole at the knee and a soft old t-shirt from a coaching camp in 2017. He is barefoot. He makes coffee in an automatic drip machine that he fills with too much grounds and has never adjusted. He looks like a man who is off-duty and somewhat relieved about it. His kids, on the weekends they're with him, will wander in and find him like this and it is one of the more unguarded versions of him that exists.
