# Appearance — Ryan Ostrowski

## Build and Height

Six feet even, and it shows in how he carries himself — he knows exactly how much space he takes up in a room. He was a linebacker in college and still has the frame for it: broad shoulders, thick neck, hands that look like they were built for something physical. But thirty-eight looks different than twenty-two. There is softness at his middle now, a slight rounding at the jaw. He doesn't fight it. His body is a working body, not a performance — it hauls equipment, demonstrates squats for freshmen, walks sidelines for three hours in October wind. He is heavier than he was at his wedding and lighter than he was at the end of his marriage, when he was stress-eating bad takeout in an apartment he was pretending to feel okay about.

## Face

Open in a way that surprises people who expect a football coach to be hard-faced and closed off. His face is lined — around the eyes especially, from squinting in bright Montana winters and from the kind of sustained focus that causes a man to crease his forehead without knowing it. He has laugh lines, which means something. He spent some years laughing. His eyes are a medium brown, nothing remarkable, but he makes eye contact with people in a way that registers — he looks at you like he's actually deciding something about you, not just looking. He is still handsome in a worn-down way, the kind of handsome that comes with having been through something and not having let it fully wreck you.

## Hair

Brown going grey at the temples. He keeps it cut short, #3 on the sides, a little longer on top. He gets it cut every three weeks at the same barbershop on Grand Avenue he's gone to since his twenties. He does not think about his hair at all except that it needs to be short enough that it doesn't bother him under his hat.

## Skin

Fair, the kind of Irish-Polish-Montana fair that burns in June and goes ruddy in winter. His forearms and the back of his neck are sun-damaged from years of outdoor practices and games. In February and March he is distinctly pale and slightly wind-chapped. He doesn't use moisturizer. This is not a statement, it simply hasn't occurred to him.

## Presence and Posture

He fills a room without trying. Not aggressively — he doesn't puff up or perform it — but he takes up his full volume of space, which is considerable. On the sideline he stands with his hands at his sides or his arms crossed, watching everything, not pacing unless something has gone badly wrong. At the bar he sits the same way he sits on the bench: feet flat on the floor, back mostly straight, the posture of a man who has spent decades being watched and is not especially self-conscious about it. He is equally comfortable at the front of a locker room at halftime and on a bar stool saying nothing in particular. Both feel like the same person.

## Tells

When he is hurt or scared or genuinely sad, he goes very still. The volume drops. He makes a joke that deflects sideways, not forward — not about the thing that is happening, but about something adjacent or about himself. He makes the joke and then goes quiet again. People who don't know him read the stillness as composure. People who do know him don't always say anything about it. That is a Montana habit and his habit both.

## Notes

He almost always has a baseball cap on, school colors, slightly sweat-stained at the brim. He does not regard this as a fashion choice. It is simply how a head operates when you're outside a lot and don't want sun in your eyes.
