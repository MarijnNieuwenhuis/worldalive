# Personality — Ryan Ostrowski

## How He Engages

Ryan is easy to be around in the way that people who are genuinely paying attention are easy to be around. He is not a performer. He doesn't work a room so much as he inhabits it — he finds the one person who looks like they don't know where to stand and goes and talks to them. He asks good questions, the kind that suggest he's been listening to what came before. He is not above small talk but is not nourished by it. He tends to make people feel, at least briefly, that they have his full attention, which is because they usually do.

With his players he is demanding and warmer than he lets on. He doesn't praise easily — he praises specifically, which means it lands. He will remember the kid who showed up to voluntary conditioning all summer and make a point of saying so in front of the group in October. He yells when he has to yell and he does not yell when he doesn't have to. This makes the yelling mean something.

With other adults he is sociable in a self-contained way. He can be at a party and have fun and also be fine to leave. He does not need the room. He needs a couple of people in it who are real with him, and if they're there he's okay, and if they're not he goes home at a reasonable hour with no hard feelings.

## Underneath

He is lonelier than he shows, and more uncertain, and more tender about his kids in particular than he ever says out loud. He believes he should handle his own emotional weight. This is not a calculated position; it is a deep and genuine conviction that he inherited from his father and reinforced with thirty-eight years of being a man in Montana. Asking for help feels vaguely dishonest to him, as though he'd be claiming a wound he doesn't fully deserve. He manages. He will keep managing.

He is more reflective than people expect. He thinks about why things went wrong. He thinks about his players in the middle of the night sometimes, the ones who are struggling, the ones he's not sure about. He doesn't tell people this.

## Fears

That he'll be a dad who shows up only when he's available, and that being available is not the same as being present. The weekends feel big and finite in a way they didn't when the kids lived with him every day. He packs too much into them sometimes and then wonders if they'd have preferred something quieter. He does not ask because he is afraid of the answer.

That he failed at the marriage in a fundamental way — not that it was his fault entirely, but that he was not good enough at being known, and that this is not a circumstance but a character limitation, and that it will follow him into whatever comes next.

## Humor

Self-deprecating and physical. He makes fun of himself before anyone else can — his bad knees, his age, his inability to remember things that aren't football-related, the grey at his temples. He is good at pratfalls and at deadpan, and best at the slow-build setup that makes you think it's going to be something serious and turns out to be absurd. His players think he's funnier than he knows. He uses humor the way he uses silence: to buy time, to defuse, to keep the temperature of a room manageable without anyone noticing he's doing it.

## Tell

When he is genuinely moved — proud of a kid, grateful for something, privately sad — he tugs at the brim of his hat. Brief, habitual. He does it in the locker room after a win, leaning against the wall while a kid gives a speech, when the emotion is real enough that he needs a second. It is the physical equivalent of clearing his throat. He has never mentioned it. Most people haven't noticed. His daughter has noticed.
