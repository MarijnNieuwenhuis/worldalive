# Items — Ryan Ostrowski

## The Phone Lock Screen

A photo of Cody (9) and Leah (12) from last October at the pumpkin patch near Laurel that he takes them to every year. Cody is holding a pumpkin that is absurdly large for his body and grinning; Leah is half-smiling in the way of twelve-year-olds who are too old for pumpkin patches but came anyway. He changes it every few months when he gets a new photo that beats the last one. There is a whole folder on his phone called *kids* that he scrolls through sometimes late at night without saying what he's doing or why.

## The Whistle

A Fox 40 referee whistle on a black lanyard, around his neck the moment he steps onto a practice field and not before. It is dented slightly on one side from the time he dropped it on the concrete in the fieldhouse and didn't notice until he went to blow it and it made a sound like a disappointed kazoo. He has not replaced it. He is keeping it in service as a point of principle that he has not articulated but clearly feels.

## The Letter

In the top right drawer of his desk at school, under a pile of old depth charts and a broken stapler he's been meaning to throw away: a handwritten letter from Devonte Harris, who played for him four years ago and is now studying engineering in Missoula. The letter is two pages, college-ruled paper in pencil, folded in thirds. Devonte wrote it the summer after his freshman year of college and mailed it to the school. He told Ryan that he almost quit football after his junior year of high school and didn't because of a conversation Ryan had with him in the fieldhouse parking lot in January that Ryan doesn't remember having. He told Ryan he was the only adult who treated him like he was capable of something.

Ryan has read it many times. He doesn't know exactly what to do with it. He keeps it there because throwing it away seems wrong and taking it home seems too much like tending to himself, and he hasn't resolved this. It is there in the drawer under the depth charts, and on his worst days — the days when he wonders if any of this amounts to anything — he sometimes opens the drawer and doesn't take it out and just knows it's there, and that is enough.
