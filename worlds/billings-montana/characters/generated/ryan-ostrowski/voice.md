# Voice — Ryan Ostrowski

## Register and Volume

On the field he is loud. Not theatrical-loud, not performed authority — genuinely loud, the kind that carries across a practice field in wind without him seeming to strain. He has a coach's voice, which means it can cut through noise and be heard in the back of a gym without microphone, and also that it can drop into the low authoritative tone that means stop what you're doing and listen. Kids respond to it before they know why. There is no waver in it on the field.

Off the field, and especially one-on-one, he is quieter than you'd expect. Not shy — he can hold a conversation, he can work a room at the booster banquet if he has to — but conversationally he listens more than he talks. He asks questions. He lets silence sit for longer than most people are comfortable with, which means the other person usually fills it, which is sometimes what he wants.

## Dialect and Origin

Pure Montana. No trace of Polish from the Ostrowski side — that's three generations back and it shows only in his last name and a tendency to make pierogies at Christmas that he learned from his grandmother. He says "yeah" not "yes," drops the g from -ing words in casual speech, uses "deal" as a noun for almost any situation (*that's the deal, what's the deal, that's not how this deal works*). When he's tired or comfortable he runs words together — *yougood, howsit, noidea*. Billings is not an accent so much as a rhythm.

## Profanity

Fluent and unashamed. On the field during practice he will swear with specificity and creativity. In the classroom he does not, and the switch is effortless and total — not self-conscious, just contextual. He is not one of those coaches who performs toughness with profanity; he swears the way most people breathe, which means you stop noticing it.

## When Something Is Wrong

He gets quiet in a particular way. His sentences get shorter. He answers questions accurately but without extra words. He does not reach for language when he is hurt or scared or grieving; he reaches for tasks, for the next thing he has to do. To people who don't know him, this reads as composure, even competence under pressure. To people who know him well — his assistant coach, maybe his kids on their better days — it reads as him being somewhere far away and not saying so.

The week after Marcus died, he coached practice the next day and said nothing about it, and afterward someone asked him how he was doing and he said *fine, thanks* and walked to his truck and sat there for eleven minutes before driving home. He did not cry at school. He cried in the truck, later. He does his private things in private and his public things in public and doesn't mix them if he can help it. He does not talk about this. He would not know what he'd say.
