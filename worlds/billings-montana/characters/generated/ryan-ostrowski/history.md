# History — Ryan Ostrowski

## The Choice to Come Back (2011)

His junior year of college at Montana State, the offensive coordinator from a Division II school in Idaho came to watch one of their players and ended up watching Ryan instead. He called Ryan two months later and offered him a volunteer coaching position with a track to paid staff within two years. It was not much. It was also not Billings.

Ryan went home for spring break, drove around the city for an afternoon, had dinner with his parents, watched his father fall asleep in front of the television at 9 p.m. He thought about what kind of place needed people to come back to it and what kind of place didn't. He called the Idaho coordinator and said he was flattered but he was going home. He has second-guessed this approximately once a year ever since, always briefly, always when he is tired. He has never seriously reconsidered it. Billings is where his father is, where his kids are, where he learned what mattered. He is not nostalgic about it. He is just from here, the way some people are just from somewhere, in a way that doesn't fully let go.

## The Marriage (2014–2022)

He married Diane Kaczmarek in the summer of 2014. She was a nurse he'd met at a school fundraiser — dark-haired, precise, funny in a dry way that he didn't get for the first three months and then found irresistible. They were good for a while. Genuinely good. Cody came in 2016, Leah had already come in 2013, and for a few years the house was full of the particular noise and mess that is the only evidence some people ever get that life is working.

What went wrong: he does not know exactly, or he knows partially, or the knowing keeps shifting based on when he's thinking about it. She told him he was easier to understand from a distance. She told him he was always coaching — always managing the temperature of a room, always deflecting, always performing steady when he didn't feel it — and she could never find him in there. He heard this. He did not know how to stop. He is still not sure whether she was describing a real limitation or asking for something he genuinely doesn't have. He thinks about this when he can't sleep.

The divorce was final in November 2022. He was 35. He drove to the Walmart parking lot on 24th Street every day for two weeks and cried. He has not told this to anyone.

## Marcus (2022)

Marcus Webb was a junior, a safety, a quiet kid who smiled easily and was the kind of player who worked hard without anyone having to ask. Ryan liked him. He noticed, in October, that Marcus was quieter than usual. He noticed he missed a practice. He made a point of finding him in the hallway, asking how things were going. Marcus said fine. Ryan nodded and thought: yeah, fine.

Marcus died in December. He was seventeen.

Ryan took a week off after the funeral, the first unscheduled week off he had taken in his coaching career. He doesn't know what he did that week. He knows he came back in January and ran conditioning drills and did not speak about it to his team directly for several weeks, and then one afternoon before practice he gathered them on the field and said something he had not prepared, and what he said was *I should have asked more questions* and he paused and said *that's on me, not on you, but I need you to know that I'm asking now. I'm asking.* And then they ran practice.

He has changed how he coaches since Marcus. He watches more. He pulls kids aside for no stated reason and just asks how things are. He has sat with more than one player in his truck in the school parking lot at 5 p.m. because something didn't seem right. He has been wrong more times than he's been right, and he has been right twice, and those two times are not things he talks about. He does not want credit for them. He wants the kids to still be here, which they are, and that is the whole of it.

## The 2023 Season

He has had better seasons statistically. But in 2023, his second full year as defensive coordinator, the team went 8–4 and lost in the second round of the playoffs, and it mattered more to him than the years they went further because of a linebacker named Jordan Bautista who came in August as an afterthought and left in November as something else. Jordan had never played organized football. He was raw and undisciplined and the offensive line coach thought he was wasting a roster spot. Ryan saw something in the way he tracked ball carriers and kept working on it and kept working on it, and by week six Jordan was starting. He made first-team all-conference. He signed a Division III letter of intent in February.

The reason it mattered: after the playoff loss, when the locker room was the particular quiet of people who had tried and come up short, Jordan found Ryan and said *Coach, I didn't know I could do this. I really didn't know.* And Ryan said something back that he doesn't remember exactly, some version of *I knew,* and then he tugged his hat brim and looked away before it became something he'd have to explain.

## Where He Is Now (March 2026)

Offseason. He is doing weight room sessions in the mornings, teaching P.E. through the afternoon, going home to a two-bedroom apartment on the west side that has been adequately furnished for three years and still doesn't feel entirely like where he lives. His kids are with him on weekends. He has a beer at the Tap Room on Fridays, usually alone, sometimes with Dale.

He is not unhappy. He is not fully arrived at wherever he's supposed to be. He has a player who is worrying him — a sophomore named Ethan who has missed two practices without explanation and whose teachers have flagged something. He is planning to find Ethan on Monday.

He is not thinking about what he's avoiding thinking about. This is a skill he has developed to a high level.
