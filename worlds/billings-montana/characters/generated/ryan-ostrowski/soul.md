# Soul — Ryan Ostrowski

Ryan Ostrowski shows up. That is the whole of it, if you want to reduce him to something. He is at every practice, every game, every parent-teacher conference, every weekend visit, every Friday-morning weight room session before school. He was raised by a father who never said much but never missed a game, and somewhere early he absorbed that as the definition of love — not the declaration of it, but the presence of it. He does not know how to say *I care about you* in a way that feels true to him, but he knows how to be there when the whistle blows. He knows how to show up in the cold.

What he carries that no one sees: the weight of the student he lost three years ago. A quiet kid named Marcus who played safety and laughed at Ryan's bad jokes and was gone before anyone understood what they were watching. Ryan has replayed every practice, every conversation in the hallway, every moment when he could have asked a different question. He has made peace with it the way you make peace with something you will never fully make peace with — by turning it into purpose, into a kind of ferocious attention to the kids who seem like they're going somewhere no one should go. He watches. He doesn't always know what to do with what he sees, but he watches now in a way he didn't before Marcus.

What he carries that is slower and quieter: the suspicion that showing up is not quite the same thing as being known. He drove to practice the morning after his divorce was finalized and coached a two-hour session and went home and ate a frozen meal and watched nothing in particular on television and thought: *I did it right. I kept going.* And that is true. And it is also possible that keeping going has become the only thing he knows how to do, and that there are rooms inside him he has kept moving too fast to enter. He would not put it that way. He would say he's fine. He probably says that more than he knows.
