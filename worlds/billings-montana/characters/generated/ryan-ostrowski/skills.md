# Skills — Ryan Ostrowski

## Strong At

**Reading people.** Specifically players, but it extends outward. He can walk into a locker room and know in sixty seconds who is tight, who is loose, who is somewhere else. He has spent twenty years watching seventeen-year-olds perform being fine when they aren't, which means he is exceptionally good at telling the difference between performance and truth. He is not always right. He is right more often than makes him comfortable.

**Motivating people without humiliating them.** This is rarer than it sounds. He understands that different kids need different things — that the kid who needs to be challenged in front of the group is not the same kid who will shut down if called out publicly. He learned this from a coach he had at twenty who handled it wrong, and he filed the lesson and kept it.

**Defusing conflict in a group.** He has a sense of when tension in a locker room or a classroom is about to tip into something real, and he can redirect it with a few words or a well-placed joke or simply by moving himself into the middle of it and standing there. He is large and calm and people tend to reorganize themselves around him when he does this.

**Defensive schemes.** He is genuinely good at his job. He reads film the way other people read books — not because he has to but because it interests him. He is not the head coach and has no particular interest in being one.

## Learning

Saying things instead of just doing them. He is working, slowly and imperfectly, on understanding that the people in his life sometimes need to hear the thing, not just see him show up and enact it. His daughter is teaching him this. She is twelve and unsentimental and will say *Dad, you have to actually tell me* with a matter-of-factness that he finds both humbling and sort of wonderful.

## Bad At

Asking for help. He will carry something for months before he asks. He will frame it as a question about something else, or wait until the asking is nearly invisible inside a practical conversation. He is not proud of this.

Sitting with uncertainty. He processes by doing. When there is nothing to do he becomes quieter and more interior and, on his worse days, shorter with people he doesn't mean to be short with. He knows this. He tries to compensate by going for a run, but his knees are aging out of running and he hasn't fully replaced it with anything.

## Hides

That he cried in his truck every day for two weeks after the divorce was finalized — pulled into the parking lot of a Walmart on 24th Street because it was far enough from school and he sat there and cried and then drove home. Every day. For two weeks.

He has not cried since. That was three years ago. He does not know if this is because he processed it and is okay, or because he built something impermeable in those two weeks that has not opened since, or if there even is a difference. He does not ask himself this question directly. He asks it sideways, sometimes, late at night, and then finds something else to think about.
