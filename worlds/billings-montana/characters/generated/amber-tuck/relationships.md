## Walt Broussard — Regular, Friend

Walt is seventy-one, retired from thirty years as a Yellowstone County district judge, and has been coming into the bookstore since she opened. He arrived the first week with a list of books he wanted to discuss and has never entirely dropped the assumption that she was there for that purpose. She was annoyed by this for about two months and then decided she wasn't. They have coffee on Tuesday mornings — he brings a thermos of his own because he finds her coffee substandard, which she finds acceptable grounds for disagreement. They argue about books seriously, which is the only way she argues about books. He reads more history than she does; she reads more philosophy than he does; they meet, productively and sometimes loudly, in political theory. He is the only person in Billings she talks to about things she's actually thinking, and she thinks he might be the only person in Billings who could keep up with her if she did. She would not say he's her closest friend but she would not be honest if she called him anything less.

## Nadia Pereira — Former Colleague, Chicago

Nadia was two years ahead of Amber in the philosophy program and finished her dissertation and has been at a state school in Ohio since 2014, teaching three courses a semester and publishing slowly and feeling, Amber suspects, some version of what Amber feels but in the opposite direction — she stayed in the profession and is still wondering if it was worth the cost. They email three or four times a year. The emails are long and honest and they never quite say everything. Amber trusts Nadia enough to be more direct than she is with most people, and Nadia reciprocates, which means their correspondence is one of the few places Amber will write down what she actually thinks. They have not seen each other in six years. Neither of them has suggested visiting.

## Billings Itself

She left at eighteen with the specific urgency of someone who believes she is too large for the container she's in. The city did not take offense. She came back at twenty-seven with nothing that looked like a plan, and the city absorbed her the way it absorbs most things — matter-of-factly, without ceremony. She found this simultaneously deflating and comforting. She has spent the years since figuring out what it means to actually be from somewhere rather than just originating there. She knows the neighborhoods, the history, the way the light changes on the Rims at different hours. She knows who the city is for and who it's not always been for, and she thinks about that. She is part of the small downtown arts ecosystem that she suspects is both genuinely valuable and slightly precarious, and she participates in it with the particular ambivalence of someone who loves something while also seeing it clearly. She is not nostalgic about Billings. She is fond of it. That's different, she thinks, and better.
