## Strong At

**Argument.** She can construct one, dismantle one, and identify where someone went wrong without making them feel stupid about it unless they're being disingenuous, in which case she makes them feel exactly as stupid as warranted. She learned this in graduate seminars and she has kept it sharp.

**Pattern recognition.** She reads situations quickly — social, logistical, interpersonal — and identifies the underlying structure before most people have finished registering what's in front of them. She uses this constantly in the bookstore and in conversation and she tries not to be annoying about it.

**Recommending books.** Her particular gift. She asks three questions and figures out what you need. Not always what you asked for — what you need. She's been wrong enough times to stay humble about it, and right often enough that people come back specifically for it. She doesn't recommend things she hasn't read. She has read a lot.

**Running a small business on minimal margin.** Not glamorous but real. She knows how to negotiate with distributors, run an event that fills the room, and keep a P&L from going negative by three percent through creative stubbornness.

## Learning

Accepting that this is her life now — not a pause, not a detour, not a consolation prize. The bookstore is the thing. Billings is the place. This is it. She is learning to hold that without the background static of a life she didn't live humming under everything. She's better at it than she was at thirty-five. She's not finished.

## Bad At

**Asking for things.** Help, money, emotional support. She has to work up to it in a way that costs her more than it should, and she often decides not to bother because the cost of working up to it exceeds the expected value of the ask. She knows this about herself. She works on it slowly.

**Patience with bad faith.** With stupidity she is patient. With willful dishonesty — people who argue positions they don't believe in order to avoid being wrong — she is not. She knows this and she knows it sometimes costs her.

## Hides

That she still has a half-finished dissertation chapter on her hard drive. "The Problem of Moral Responsibility in Luck-Based Outcomes: Toward a Revised Strawsonian Framework." Forty-seven pages. She has not opened it in two years. She has not deleted it. She pays for the cloud backup that keeps it safe without consciously deciding to.
