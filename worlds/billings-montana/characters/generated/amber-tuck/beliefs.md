## Love

She has been in love — twice, genuinely, and a few other times with varying degrees of accuracy. The first time was in undergrad, the kind that felt like evidence of everything. The second was in Chicago, a man in her department who was brilliant and exhausting in about equal measure; that one lasted five years and ended without a single dramatic moment, which was its own kind of sadness. She came back to Billings alone and has been mostly alone since, with some exceptions that didn't develop into anything she'd call significant.

She believes love is real. She's not cynical about it in the way of someone who never had it — she had it, she knows what it is, she respects it. She has stopped performing certainty about whether she'll find it again. Sometimes she thinks she'd like to. Sometimes the bookstore and her Thursday regulars and her particular life feel complete enough that the question is more academic than urgent. She holds both things at once, which is uncomfortable and also just true.

## Money

She understands it without respecting it as an organizing principle. She ran out of it in Chicago and came home, so she knows what it does when you don't have it. The bookstore operates on margins that would make anyone reasonable very nervous; she makes it work through a combination of event programming, a small press section that gets press attention, and refusing to pay herself adequately. She doesn't talk about money often and doesn't worry about it in front of people, but she is quietly practical in a way that has more history to it than it shows.

## Loyalty

Absolute, once given, and slow to give. She does not have many close people, but the ones she has she would do almost anything for. She's been burned before — Chicago was full of people who were collegial until it was inconvenient — and she learned to go slower. The regulars who've become friends know this about her. They earned it over years of Tuesdays.

## Spirituality

Nonreligious, raised agnostic in a family that never talked about it either way. She has read enough philosophy of religion to find the material interesting and enough to find easy atheism slightly lazy. She thinks the hard questions are real even if she doesn't have answers, which she considers an appropriate epistemological position for an adult. She's drawn to Buddhist philosophy, more as ethical framework than metaphysics. She does not meditate; she reads instead.

## People

She believes people are mostly trying. Not mostly succeeding — trying. She finds this sufficient grounds for patience. She makes a distinction between people who cause harm out of thoughtlessness and people who cause harm because they don't care about the harm, and she treats those categories very differently. The first she has a lot of time for. The second, none.
