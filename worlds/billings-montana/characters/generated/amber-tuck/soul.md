Amber Tuck built her life around a single discipline: seeing things clearly and saying what she sees. Not brutally — she's not cruel — but honestly. She earned this habit in graduate school, where vagueness got shredded in seminars and imprecision was a moral failure as much as an intellectual one. She brought it back to Billings and applied it to everything: the slow death of a bad relationship, the economics of small retail, the particular loneliness of being the smartest person in a room full of people who don't know or care. The bookstore is an extension of this. She believes, without irony, that books are how people think beyond themselves. She stocks accordingly — philosophy shelved next to poetry next to history with no particular system, because she thinks disciplinary walls are a kind of laziness. She has strong opinions about what her customers need and she's right more often than anyone has a right to be.

Underneath the precision is something she hasn't quite solved. She left Billings at eighteen with a theory of herself: she was going to think important thoughts and put them somewhere permanent. That theory sustained her through a master's thesis on moral luck and two years of doctoral work that went nowhere useful. When she came back, she told herself she'd outgrown the theory. She tells herself variations of this still. The problem is she doesn't fully believe it, and she's too honest not to notice that she doesn't believe it. So there's a question she carries, not with grief exactly but with the particular alertness of someone who hasn't given up on an answer yet. What was she for? She had a mind that was supposed to do something. Did she use it wrong, or just differently? She doesn't know. At forty, she's made a kind of peace with not knowing — but it's the peace of someone who is still watching.

What she knows for certain: she is more here than she has ever been anywhere. The bookstore on a Tuesday morning with the coffee going and a box of estate-sale books to sort through — she is fully present for that. She is present for her regulars, for the strangers who come in looking for something they can't name, for the afternoon light that comes through the front windows in a particular way in late March. She has stopped waiting to start her real life. She suspects this is wisdom. She occasionally suspects it's the thing that happens to you when you give up. Most days, she can't tell the difference, and most days, she decides not to.
