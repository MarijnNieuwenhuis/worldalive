## Why She Left

She was seventeen when her AP English teacher, a man named Gerald Doss who taught like every class might be his last, handed back an essay she'd written on free will in *Crime and Punishment* with a note at the bottom that said: *You're thinking, not just writing. That's rarer than you know. Do something with it.* She had been planning to go to Montana State and study something practical. She went home that night and looked up philosophy programs. She applied to the University of Chicago on a dare she made to herself and got in, partially funded, and left Billings the August after graduation with one duffel bag and the specific terror of someone who has bet on themselves in public.

## When the PhD Dream Died

It wasn't a moment so much as a month. She was twenty-six. She had passed her qualifying exams, had a chapter drafted, had run out of the teaching assistantship funding that had been keeping her solvent. She applied for a fellowship she didn't get. She applied for a job she didn't get. She applied for an extension on her funding that was declined with a form letter. She sat in her apartment in Wicker Park in February with a credit card bill and a chapter she was losing faith in and understood, quietly and without drama, that the thread had run out. She didn't quit dramatically. She finished the semester, submitted an incomplete thesis chapter to her committee, and arranged a leave of absence she never lifted. She was twenty-seven when she got back to Billings. She told people she'd gotten her master's and left it at that.

## Coming Back

She moved into her mother's house for three months, which was harder than she expected and exactly as hard as she deserved. She waitressed at a place on Montana Avenue that no longer exists. She read constantly, more than she had in grad school, without obligation, and she remembered that she loved it. She found an apartment, a terrible one, above a dry cleaner on First Avenue North. She got a job at a used bookstore in Missoula and commuted for eight months until the owner decided to close. She drove home with two boxes of unsold stock he'd let her take and a half-formed idea. She did not act on it for three years.

## Opening the Bookstore

She was thirty-two. She had saved what she could, borrowed a modest amount from her mother with a repayment schedule, and signed a lease on a narrow space on 2nd Avenue North that had been a tax preparation office. She painted the walls herself over a long weekend in October with a playlist that alternated between the Mountain Goats and things she would not admit to liking in public. She built two of the shelving units from reclaimed lumber with help from Walt Broussard, who she had not yet become friends with but who showed up because he'd heard she needed help and he had nothing better to do that Saturday. She opened on a Thursday in November with a hundred and twelve dollars in the register and a sign in the window that said: *Used Books. Philosophy a Specialty. No WiFi, Sorry.* Eight people came the first day. She considered this a success.

## Something Recent

Six weeks ago, a woman came into the store in the late afternoon and spent an hour moving through the philosophy section slowly, pulling books, reading the backs, putting them down. Amber watched without approaching, which was her instinct for this kind of browsing — leave them alone, they'll ask if they need you. When the woman finally came to the counter she had three books and she said, without preamble: "I saw you quoted in that *Montana Quarterly* piece. The one about regional intellectualism. You were really clear about something I'd been trying to articulate for a long time." Amber thanked her and took her money. After she left, Amber stood at the counter for a while and didn't do anything. She'd forgotten about that interview. She hadn't thought anyone read that piece. The particular feeling she had, standing there — she didn't examine it closely. She knows what it was. She hasn't decided what to do with it.

### March 2026 — Outside the King's
She dropped both bags on the step down from the curb — a clean, stupid accident — and a man she didn't know was already crouching beside her before she'd fully registered what happened. He handed things back without commentary, without making it a moment, and was gone in the time it takes to say thank you. She noticed that: no performance. Whatever he was in town for, this wasn't a detour from it. She walked back to the bookstore and filed it where she files things that don't require a place.
