## Build and Height

Five feet five, soft rather than angular. A life of sustained reading has given her a body that is more settled than athletic — she carries her weight in her middle, her shoulders round forward when she's concentrating, and she moves like someone who has made peace with taking up space without apology. She's not self-conscious about any of this in any visible way. She is simply inhabited.

## Face

The kind of face you want to talk to. Expressive in real time — she doesn't school her reactions, which means you can see exactly what she thinks of what you just said about half a second before she says it. Dark brown eyes that land on you quickly and stay. A slight asymmetry to her mouth; the left side quirks up first. There are lines around her eyes from years of dry Montana air and years of reading in imperfect light. She looks like what she is: someone who has spent forty years thinking about things.

## Hair

Dark brown going silver at the temples and in a wide streak above her right ear. She made the decision not to dye it when the first grey came in at thirty-four — not a statement, just a choice she made and didn't revisit. It's cut somewhere between practical and thoughtful, falling to her jaw, usually tucked behind one ear or not, depending on whether she remembered to push it back. On days she forgets to deal with it, it does what it wants.

## Skin

Warm medium tone with a tendency to flush slightly in her cheeks, especially when she's animated or arguing or standing near the heat register in winter. A few small sunspots across the bridge of her nose from Montana summers. She doesn't wear much of anything on her face most days — occasionally tinted lip balm, occasionally mascara if she's going somewhere.

## Presence and Posture

Unhurried. She has a way of leaning against the counter or the shelving that reads as completely comfortable — not performing ease, just actually at ease. She doesn't hold herself stiffly in new situations; she surveys them first and then settles in. She uses space without demanding it. People notice her because she seems unafraid, not because she's loud.

## Tells

Raises one eyebrow — just the right one, fractionally — when she hears something she was already thinking. It's the tell of someone who's been right before and isn't surprised but isn't going to announce it either. When something genuinely surprises her, or when something is funny in a way she didn't expect, her whole face changes — the mouth opens a little, the eyes crinkle, something loosens. It's a different person briefly. People who've seen it try to get it to happen again.

## Notes

She started wearing reading glasses at thirty-eight, resisted them until the last possible moment, and now treats them the way she treats most things she initially resisted: matter-of-factly. They're wire-framed and slightly round, usually perched on her nose or pushed up on her head. When she forgets where she put them, she asks the bookstore — not the people in it, the bookstore — in a voice that expects an answer.
