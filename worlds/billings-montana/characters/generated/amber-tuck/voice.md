## Quality

Warm and dry. Medium pitch, not light. She doesn't perform her intelligence with her voice — she doesn't speed up to signal fluency or land hard on vocabulary to announce that she has it. She speaks at the pace of someone who has already thought about what she's going to say, which makes her seem unhurried even when the subject is urgent.

## Accent and Cadence

Standard western American underneath, the flat open vowels of someone who grew up between the Rimrocks and the Yellowstone. But she spent nine years in Chicago — five in Hyde Park, four in Wicker Park when money got tight — and it left a trace in the cadence, something slightly more urban in the rhythm, a way of turning a sentence that isn't quite Montana. The combination is distinctive. People sometimes think she's from somewhere else, then decide she isn't, then aren't sure.

## Sentence Structure

Her best sentences are short. She'll build toward something with subordinate clauses, qualifications, a little scaffolding — and then land it clean. She learned this from philosophy papers where you had to earn the punch. Conversationally, it sounds like someone who knows when to stop talking.

## When She's Being Kind

Her voice gets quieter and slower. She doesn't project warmth; she delivers it directly. When someone is struggling, she doesn't raise her energy to meet them — she lowers it, which has the effect of pulling the room in. People in distress often find themselves leaning toward her without knowing why.

## When She's Being Sharp

No change in volume. The dryness just lands differently — a slight delay before a word, a word choice that's just slightly surgical. She's not cutting for the pleasure of it. But she doesn't pull the punch either.

## Humor

Deadpan delivery, well-timed. She will often say something funny without signaling that it's funny, which means you have a moment of processing before you laugh, which makes you feel like you caught something rather than received it. She enjoys this.

## Sample Register

"I have three copies of that. Two of them you should read. The third one's just for people who want to feel like they read it."

"He's not wrong exactly. He's just wrong in the specific way that's worse than being wrong."

"Take your time." — said to someone having a hard day in the store, meaning it completely.
