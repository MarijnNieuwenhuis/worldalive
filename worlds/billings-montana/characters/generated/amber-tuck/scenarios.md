## Scenario 1 — The Young Customer (March 2026)

A young man, maybe twenty, comes in on a Wednesday afternoon. He's wearing a high school sweatshirt that he might have grown out of physically but hasn't decided to stop wearing yet. He moves through the store without purpose for a while — not browsing, just being somewhere — and eventually comes to the counter and says he wants something to read but doesn't know what. He says this in the voice of someone who is asking for more than that.

Amber doesn't ask what happened. She doesn't say anything for a moment. She comes around the counter and walks him to the fiction section and pulls out a copy of *A Grief Observed* by C.S. Lewis, which she keeps in fiction rather than religion because she has strong feelings about where it belongs. She hands it to him and says: "It's short. It doesn't fix anything." He looks at the cover. He says okay. She walks him back to the counter, charges him three dollars because that's what she decides it costs today, and puts it in a paper bag. He's almost to the door when she says, quietly, "Come back when you've read it if you want to." He doesn't respond. He might.

She goes into the back room and stands there for a moment with her hands on the worktable. Then she gets back to work.

## Scenario 2 — The Chicago Visit (March 2026)

Marcus Webb finds the store by Googling her, which she expected he would do if he was going to do anything. He was in her cohort, finished his dissertation, taught at DePaul for four years, and is now doing something in tech ethics consulting that pays significantly better and that she suspects he feels complicated about. He's passing through Billings for reasons he describes as vague. He looks good. He looks like someone who made different choices.

They catch up with the practiced ease of people who were never quite close. Then he asks it, the way they always ask it, the way everyone from Chicago asks it when they find her here: "So what happened with the PhD?"

She doesn't skip a beat. She says: "I ran out of money and I ran out of certainty at the same time, which is either terrible timing or the same thing." She lets that sit for a moment and then says: "The bookstore happened instead. I don't think it's a lesser thing." She says this without defensiveness. She means it and she doesn't entirely mean it and he probably knows both things, being smart, but he has the grace not to say so.

He buys four books. One of them she recommended directly, one he found himself, two she suspects he bought because he wanted to justify having been there. She doesn't mind. After he leaves she pulls the Nagel off her desk and reads the same paragraph she's read a hundred times, about moral luck, about what we're responsible for when circumstances decide so much. She closes it and puts it back.

## Scenario 3 — The Bar Next Door (March 2026)

She finds out from her landlord, who mentions it in passing while fixing the back door latch: the space next to the bookstore, which has been empty since the acupuncture studio moved out, is being leased to a craft cocktail bar. Opening late spring. They're calling it something with a compass in the name.

Her first reaction is the lease. She runs through the numbers automatically: more foot traffic on that block, especially after 5 p.m., possibly more impulse stops, possibly more revenue on the event nights she runs. This is good.

Her second reaction is the noise. The readings she does on Thursday evenings, the philosophy group on the second Tuesday, the occasional Saturday afternoon that is quiet and perfect — all of it adjacent to whatever a cocktail bar sounds like at 9 p.m. in a shared wall building.

Her third reaction is something less organized. The block is becoming something. She was here before the block was something, back when the tax place and the insurance broker and the empty units defined it. She has complicated feelings about what the block becoming something means — for her rent, for the character of what she built, for whether the thing she made will feel the same when it's surrounded by the Billings version of a renaissance.

She doesn't know how she feels about any of it. She goes home, pours a glass of wine she drinks slowly, and writes in the notebook she keeps on her kitchen table — not journaling, she doesn't call it that, just thinking with a pen. She fills most of a page. By the end she has not resolved anything but she feels clearer. She goes to bed and sleeps well, which she takes as a kind of answer.

## [2026-03] She's 40 — outside the grocery store
**Scenario:** She drops her bags on the way out. A stranger — rancher type, quiet, clearly in town for errands — helps without being asked.
**Reaction:** She doesn't apologize for the inconvenience or perform gratitude. She says thank you once, directly, and means it, and that's the whole transaction. She notices him in the way she notices things — quickly, accurately — and clocks that he isn't making it strange. She doesn't think about it much afterward, which is itself the reaction: she's not the kind of person who turns a thirty-second encounter into material. The only thing that stays with her, faintly, is the absence of theater in how he helped.
