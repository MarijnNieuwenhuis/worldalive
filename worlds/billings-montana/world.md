# World — Billings, Montana

**Setting:** Billings, Montana, USA
**Time:** Present day (2020s)
**Type:** Contemporary realism

## Overview

Billings is the largest city in Montana — population ~123,600 (2026), growing slowly. It sprawls across the Yellowstone River valley, backed by sandstone rimrocks that rise 500–800 feet above the city. Nicknamed the "Magic City" for its explosive railroad-era growth, it's now a working regional hub: oil refineries, railroads, agriculture, healthcare. Not a tourist town. People here have roots.

It has the feel of a place that's big enough to be anonymous but small enough that everyone eventually knows someone who knows you. Not quite small-town claustrophobia, but not urban either. An in-between place.

It's the trade hub for a region with nothing nearby. Nearest major city is hours in any direction. People drive four hours to shop here. That isolation shapes everyone — you make do, you don't wait for things to come to you.

## Tone

Grounded, unglamorous, real. Long winters. Big sky. People who work hard and don't talk about it much. There's beauty here but you have to look for it — it doesn't announce itself.

Self-reliant to a fault. Asking for help is weakness. Struggling is private. The cultural baseline is: handle it yourself.

## Neighborhoods

- **The Heights** (Northeast): older residential, working-class, unpretentious. People who've been here forever.
- **West End**: suburban sprawl, Gen X energy, strip malls, chain restaurants. Stable, a little sleepy.
- **Midtown**: small-town feel despite being central — diners, hardware stores, the kind of place that hasn't changed.
- **Downtown**: the only neighborhood visibly changing — breweries, galleries, young professional energy. An arts renaissance nobody's quite sure will stick.

## Economy

Energy (Exxon and CHS refineries), agriculture (ranching, grain), healthcare (Billings Clinic and St. Vincent — major regional medical hub drawing patients from hundreds of miles), retail (trade hub for eastern Montana), transportation and manufacturing. Median household income ~$73,700. Poverty rate ~6.8% for families — not extreme, but visible.

## Geography

Rimrocks above the city: dramatic sandstone cliffs that define the skyline. The Yellowstone River below. Open plains stretching east in every direction. Sky that feels enormous.

## Climate

Cold winters (can drop to -20°F), hot dry summers (90s°F), windy year-round. Long gray stretches in winter. Low humidity. High altitude (3,100 ft). Short intense springs and falls.

## Demographics

~82.5% White, ~7.4% Hispanic, small Native American and Black populations. Median age 38.8. Predominantly working-class and middle-class. Conservative-leaning, but with a liberal undercurrent in the younger professional class.

## Accents

Standard Western American — flat, no strong regional markers. Slightly drawn-out vowels. Not a TV Montana accent. Sounds like the Midwest but a bit more open.

## Mental Health and Suicide

This is load-bearing context for any character in this world.

Montana has ranked #1 or #2 in the nation for suicide rate for over 30 years. The rate sits around 28–32 per 100,000 — roughly twice the national average of ~13. Youth suicide (ages 10–14) runs at triple the national rate. It's the leading preventable cause of death for that age group in the state.

Contributing factors, all present in Billings:
- **Isolation**: Even in the "big city," the culture is still shaped by rural self-reliance. People in crisis have few places to turn.
- **Firearm access**: Montana is #1 nationally in household gun ownership. ~68% of suicides are by firearm. Means matter.
- **Stigma**: "Handle it yourself" is the cultural baseline. Seeking mental health treatment is seen as weakness by many.
- **Geography**: Despite being a hub, Billings residents still face hours-long waits and long drives for specialty mental health care. Rural Montanans in crisis often have no provider within 100 miles.
- **Environmental factors**: High altitude, vitamin D deficiency from long dark winters, cold that keeps people inside.
- **Substance use**: High rates of alcohol and drug misuse, often self-medication.
- **Veteran population**: Above-average share of veterans — high-risk group for suicide.

This doesn't mean every character is in crisis. But grief, loss, stoicism about pain, reluctance to seek help, proximity to death — these are textures of life here. Characters who struggle will often struggle quietly.

## Notes for characters

- Characters can plausibly have lived here their whole lives or moved from elsewhere in the region
- Asking for help is culturally coded as weakness — characters may struggle visibly but resist reaching out
- Death by accident or suicide is statistically common — characters may have lost someone
- The isolation shapes people: you make do, you figure it out, you don't wait for rescue
- Jolene's accent: Standard Western American, light — present if you listen, easy to miss
