# Event: Dropped Groceries

**Date:** March 2026
**Location:** Outside a grocery store, downtown Billings (near 1st Ave N — close to Amber's bookstore)
**Characters affected:** Amber Tuck, Cole Brandt

## What happened

The door swung out and Amber got through it without trouble — it was the step down from the curb that did it, the right bag catching the edge of her hip as she shifted her weight, and then the paper handles were gone and both bags were on the sidewalk, one of them spilling. A can of chickpeas rolled three feet and stopped against a parking meter. A lemon went the other direction. She stood there for a half-second with her hands open, looking at it, and said quietly, to no one: "Of course."

He was already moving. She registered him peripherally — a man in a canvas work jacket, unhurried in the way that meant he knew what he was doing — and by the time she'd crouched for the nearest bag he was crouching too, picking up what had rolled, unhurried still, no theater about it. He got the can of chickpeas. He got the lemon. He put them back in the upright bag without any particular ceremony, stood, and held it out.

She took it. "Thank you," she said. Not warm, not clipped — proportional. She looked at him directly and he looked back at her in the same register: two people occupying the same moment without making anything of it.

He nodded once. He'd already assessed the situation and resolved it; there was nothing left that needed his attention. He turned and that was it — back to whatever had brought him to this block in the first place, his stride the same going as it had been coming, no residue of the interaction. She stood a moment longer, bags in hand, reorganizing her grip. The can of chickpeas had a small dent in it now that it hadn't had before. She looked at it briefly, decided it didn't matter, and walked. She had twenty minutes before she needed to be back at the store.

She passed him once more at the corner — he was at the truck, doing something with something in the truck bed, not looking up — and she didn't slow, and he didn't look up, and they went in different directions entirely.

## Ripple effects

Amber walks back to the bookstore thinking about chickpeas, then not thinking about anything in particular. For a few minutes, in a way she couldn't explain if asked, she is mildly aware that she is walking. The steadiness of the help — no commentary, no performance of helpfulness — registered somewhere she doesn't examine. By the time she unlocks the back door and puts the bags on the worktable, she's thinking about something else.

Cole completes his errands and gets back on the highway before noon. At some point on the drive — not a specific point, just somewhere in the flat stretch before the turnoff — he finds himself thinking about nothing at all, which is as close to pleasant as a long drive gets. The woman with the groceries doesn't come to mind again. Something in the exchange was frictionless in a way that doesn't leave a mark. He doesn't think about the brevity of it. He doesn't think about it.
